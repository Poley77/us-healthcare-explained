Every chapter in this book has examined one piece of the US healthcare system. This chapter puts them all together.

What follows is not a summary. It is a map — a structured view of every major actor in the ecosystem, how they connect to each other, what flows between them, and where the value-based care transformation is reshaping those connections. The goal is to give you a single reference point that you can return to as you encounter new organizations, new contracts, and new acronyms in your work.

## How to Read It

The map is organized around four layers, read from top to bottom: the payer layer (where money originates), the accountability layer (where risk is accepted and distributed), the infrastructure layer (where operational support is delivered), and the care delivery layer (where clinical work happens). Arrows represent contractual relationships and payment flows. The thickness of a connection reflects how central that relationship is to the economics of value-based care.

Colors correspond to the layer system used throughout this book: payer relationships in blue, risk and accountability in orange, enablement and infrastructure in purple, data flows in green. Organizations that operate across multiple layers — like UnitedHealth Group, which spans payer, care delivery, analytics, and pharmacy — appear at the intersection of those layers.

Use this map as a compass when you encounter a new organization, a new acronym, or a new policy development. Start by asking: which layer does this sit in? What flows through it? Who is it accountable to, and who is accountable to it?

## The Four Flows

The US healthcare system is best understood not as a hierarchy but as a network through which four things move simultaneously.

**Money** flows from payers — CMS, employers, individuals — through health plans, into ACOs and IPAs, down to physician practices and hospitals, and ultimately to the employees, vendors, and investors who depend on those organizations. In <T>fee-for-service</T>, money moves reactively in response to services rendered. In value-based care, money moves proactively as fixed capitated payments, then retroactively as shared savings.

**Risk** flows in the opposite direction. CMS takes on the actuarial risk of the Medicare population, then transfers a portion to MA plans through risk-adjusted capitation. MA plans transfer risk downward to ACOs and risk-bearing entities. ACOs pass risk to IPAs. IPAs pass it to physician practices — often retaining stop-loss protection to cap the exposure. Each layer accepts risk in exchange for an opportunity to earn more than it spends.

**Data** flows in every direction, though imperfectly and with significant lag. Claims flow from providers to payers and back. EHR data flows from practices to analytics platforms. ADT feeds flow from hospitals to care management teams. RAF scores flow from coders to CMS. Quality measure data flows from providers to plans and ACOs and ultimately to CMS for Stars ratings and shared savings determinations.

**Care** flows from patient need to the appropriate clinical setting — ideally managed proactively by a primary care team, coordinated across specialists, and supported by care managers who can intervene before crises escalate. The entire infrastructure of VBC exists to make this flow more effective.

## The Payer Layer

At the top of the funding structure sits the federal government, through <T>CMS</T>. CMS administers <T>traditional Medicare</T> (Parts A and B) on a fee-for-service basis for roughly 30 million beneficiaries, and contracts with private <T>Medicare Advantage</T> plans to cover another 33 million on a capitated basis. It also sets federal Medicaid rules, funds CHIP, runs the MSSP and ACO REACH programs, and operates CMMI — the innovation center that pilots alternative payment models before scaling them.

Private health plans sit one layer below CMS in the MA structure, but operate independently in the commercial and Medicaid markets. The largest — UnitedHealthcare, Humana, CVS Health/Aetna, Elevance Health, Centene — each cover tens of millions of lives across multiple product lines. Commercial plans collect employer-funded premiums and manage medical costs within a regulated Medical Loss Ratio. MA plans collect risk-adjusted CMS payments and earn Stars bonuses. Medicaid MCOs receive state-managed capitation payments and must navigate the complex political economy of state Medicaid policy.

Self-insured employers — roughly 65% of employer-sponsored coverage by enrollment — do not transfer risk to an insurer. They pay claims directly, hiring commercial plans as <T>Administrative Services Only</T> administrators. This gives large employers significant leverage over plan design, network construction, and VBC contracting.

## The Accountability Layer

Between payers and providers sits the accountability layer: the organizations that accept contractual responsibility for population health outcomes and distribute that responsibility downward.

<T>ACO</T>s are the primary accountability mechanism in Medicare. An MSSP ACO signs a participation agreement with CMS, accepts a benchmark for its attributed population's total cost of care, and earns shared savings if it performs below that benchmark while meeting quality thresholds. ACO REACH operates similarly but with greater risk flexibility and broader eligibility. In the MA market, ACOs contract directly with health plans, accepting <T>sub-capitation</T> payments and managing care within that budget.

<T>IPA</T>s aggregate independent physicians into contracting networks. They receive sub-capitation or shared savings distributions from ACOs and distribute them to member practices. IPAs vary enormously in sophistication — some are simple contracting shells; others operate sophisticated care management programs, analytics platforms, and quality improvement infrastructure. The IPA is often the first place a physician practice encounters real VBC accountability.

Risk-bearing entities — like agilon health and Alignment Health — go further, accepting full global capitation from MA plans and taking on the actuarial risk of the entire attributed population's cost of care.

## The Infrastructure Layer

The accountability layer only functions because of the infrastructure layer beneath it: the organizations that provide the operational, technological, and financial capabilities that make population health management possible.

<T>MSO</T>s are the most prevalent infrastructure players. They provide billing and revenue cycle management, analytics and population health platforms, care management staffing, quality reporting, payer contracting support, and financial management to physician practices, IPAs, and ACOs. The MSO model solves the Corporate Practice of Medicine constraint: MSOs can be investor-owned and operate at scale, while the clinical entity (the professional corporation) remains physician-controlled.

Analytics vendors, EHR platforms, pharmacy benefit managers, and care management technology companies all contribute to the infrastructure layer. The most sophisticated VBC organizations have integrated these capabilities internally — Optum operates as both insurer and analytics provider; Alignment Health runs its own data platform alongside its MA plan and physician employment model.

## The Care Delivery Layer

At the point of care, the ecosystem reduces to a set of human relationships: between patients and physicians, between primary care and specialist, between the care coordinator and the high-risk patient at home.

Primary care physicians are the strategic center of the VBC ecosystem. Their documentation of patient diagnoses drives RAF scores. Their management of chronic disease drives quality measure performance. Their relationships with patients drive attribution — patients tend to follow their primary care physician into whichever contracting arrangement the physician joins. The VBC system is, in a fundamental sense, an attempt to give primary care physicians the infrastructure, incentives, and data to do their job more effectively.

Specialists, hospitals, and post-acute providers are the major cost drivers that primary care-centered VBC is designed to manage. Reducing avoidable hospitalizations, preventing 30-day readmissions, directing appropriate referrals to high-value specialists — these are the interventions that move the needle on total cost of care. The challenge is that specialists and hospitals have their own economic incentives (FFS payment for volume) that are frequently misaligned with VBC's goal of reducing utilization.

## The Contracts That Hold It Together

Every relationship in this ecosystem is governed by a contract. The nested contract chain runs: CMS → health plan → ACO → IPA → physician practice → MSO. Each contract passes down obligations (quality targets, data sharing, care management expectations) and passes up accountability (performance reporting, attribution data, cost tracking).

The key terms that appear throughout this chain — <T>benchmark</T>, <T>shared savings</T>, <T>minimum savings rate</T>, <T>attribution</T>, <T>stop-loss</T>, <T>clawback</T>, carve-outs — are not administrative details. They are the economic structure of the relationship, and understanding them is what separates a sophisticated participant in VBC from one who is simply executing tasks without understanding why.

## Where Value-Based Care Has Taken Hold — and Where It Hasn't

Value-based care is not evenly distributed across the healthcare system. It is concentrated in specific programs, specific markets, and specific patient populations.

Medicare Advantage is the largest and most developed VBC market. With over 33 million members, mandatory risk adjustment, Stars quality incentives, and a regulatory framework that rewards sophisticated care management, MA has attracted the most capital, the most innovation, and the most organizational complexity. The ecosystem described in this book — ACOs, IPAs, MSOs, risk-bearing entities, analytics platforms — has grown up primarily in and around MA.

Traditional Medicare through MSSP is the second major VBC arena. MSSP ACOs cover tens of millions of attributed beneficiaries. Performance has been variable — some ACOs generate consistent savings; many do not — but the program has created durable infrastructure and a generation of VBC-experienced organizations.

Commercial VBC — employer-sponsored plans with value-based provider contracts — exists and is growing, but more slowly. Commercial contracting is more fragmented, the quality measurement frameworks are less standardized, and the regulatory mandates that have driven MA adoption do not apply in the same way.

Medicaid VBC is the most variable and politically contingent of the major markets. State-by-state variation in managed care program design, rate-setting, and VBC requirements creates a patchwork that makes national scale difficult to achieve.

## The System's Central Tension

Every map needs a legend — a key to what you're actually looking at. The legend for the US healthcare ecosystem is a single tension that runs through every layer.

Fee-for-service creates incentives for volume. Value-based care creates incentives for outcomes. Both operate simultaneously, in the same organizations, for the same physicians, with the same patients. The result is a system in which every actor is simultaneously navigating two different sets of incentives — and the organizations that succeed are the ones that have built infrastructure sophisticated enough to optimize for both while maintaining the cash flow stability to survive the transition.

The transition is not complete. Most US healthcare spending still flows through FFS mechanisms. Most US physicians still generate the majority of their income from FFS billing. The ACOs and MSOs and risk-bearing entities described in this book are real and growing, but they operate at the edge of a system that remains predominantly organized around volume.

Whether the transition completes — whether VBC becomes the dominant payment model rather than a growing but still-minority alternative — depends on regulatory choices, capital flows, clinical workforce decisions, and ultimately on whether the people doing the work of healthcare are given the infrastructure, incentives, and support they need to deliver better care at lower cost.

That is the question this system is still trying to answer.
