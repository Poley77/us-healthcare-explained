The previous two chapters examined organizations that built VBC platforms through physician partnerships, joint ventures, and integrated care delivery. Aledade and Privia enable independent physicians. agilon and Alignment bear full insurance risk. All four are defined primarily by their clinical models — the way they organize care.

This chapter examines something different: the financial model that VBC made possible, and the industry it spawned.

When <T>value-based care</T> programs created a reliable, recurring, government-backed revenue stream tied to managing Medicare patients well, they did something that market participants noticed immediately. They turned a fragmented collection of independent physician practices — historically difficult to invest in, nearly impossible to scale through conventional acquisition — into a predictable, high-value asset class. And private equity moved in.

---

## Why VBC Created an Investable Asset

To understand the wave of private equity investment that flowed into primary care over the 2010s and 2020s, you need to understand what changed when <T>Medicare Advantage</T> reached scale.

Under traditional <T>fee-for-service</T> medicine, a primary care physician's economics are structurally constrained. Revenue is a function of volume — the number of appointments, procedures, and visits that can be billed in a day. Margins are thin. Growth requires hiring more physicians. The asset is the physician relationship, which cannot be consolidated the way a factory or retail chain can.

<T>Medicare Advantage</T> changed that equation. Under MA, a primary care physician managing a panel of Medicare patients generates a <T>capitation</T> payment — a fixed monthly amount per patient, adjusted for how sick those patients are. If the physician manages that population effectively — keeping people out of the hospital, closing care gaps, accurately coding diagnoses — the capitated revenue exceeds the cost of care. That surplus is the profit. And unlike FFS, it scales with the size of the patient panel, not the number of daily appointments.

The critical insight for investors: the value in this model is the patient panel. A physician practice with 2,000 attributed Medicare Advantage patients, a high <T>RAF score</T>, and strong quality metrics is not just a medical practice. It is a risk-bearing asset generating predictable monthly cash flows from the federal government. Assemble enough of those practices under common management infrastructure, and you have something institutional investors can value, finance, and eventually sell.

The only obstacle was legal.

## The CPOM Workaround: Why the MSO Structure Exists

In most US states, corporations cannot own medical practices or directly employ physicians to practice medicine. This is the <T>Corporate Practice of Medicine</T> doctrine — a legal principle rooted in the concern that clinical decisions should be made by physicians exercising independent judgment, not by business executives optimizing financial performance.

For private equity, this created an obvious problem. You cannot simply acquire a physician practice the way you might acquire a restaurant chain or a software company. The physicians must retain nominal ownership of the clinical entity.

The <T>Management Services Organization</T> is the legal architecture that solved this problem.

<Diagram id="ch20_img01" />

In a typical PE-backed MSO structure, the investor does not buy the physician practice. Instead, the investor creates or acquires a non-clinical MSO entity — a management company. The MSO then enters into a long-term <T>Management Services Agreement</T> with a physician-owned <T>Professional Corporation</T>. Under that agreement, the MSO takes over everything non-clinical: the clinic buildings (owned or leased by the MSO), the IT infrastructure, the billing and coding operations, the payer contracts, the HR functions, the care management programs. The physicians continue practicing under their own PC, technically employed by themselves, technically independent. The MSO charges the PC a management fee — typically a percentage of revenue, or a fixed monthly fee, or both — that captures the economic value.

This structure is legal. It is widely used. And it is, in practice, a mechanism through which a private equity firm can control the economics of a physician practice without violating the letter of CPOM laws.

The distinction between "controlling the economics" and "owning the practice" is real but narrow. The MSO typically holds long-term, exclusive management agreements with the PC that are difficult to exit. The MSO owns the physical assets, the payer contracts, and in some cases the intellectual property of the practice. The physician-owner of the PC may hold equity that looks significant on paper but reflects little independent control. Critics of this structure argue that it achieves the functional equivalent of corporate medicine ownership under the technical protection of physician ownership. Supporters argue that it enables capital investment in primary care that would otherwise be impossible, and that the resulting infrastructure — analytics platforms, care management programs, population health capabilities — directly benefits patients.

Both things are true. The structure creates genuine capabilities that independent practices cannot build alone. It also concentrates economic control in non-physician hands in ways the CPOM doctrine was designed to prevent. That tension is ongoing, actively litigated in some states, and increasingly the subject of regulatory scrutiny.

## The MaxHealth Story: A Textbook Roll-Up

MaxHealth is among the clearest illustrations of how private equity deploys the MSO structure to build scale in value-based primary care. Its creation, growth, and eventual sale followed a pattern that private equity has replicated across dozens of markets — and understanding the sequence reveals both the sophistication of the model and the logic that drives it.

### The Foundation: Best Value Healthcare (2018–2020)

The story begins not with MaxHealth, but with a much smaller operation. In 2018, Kimberly Ficocelli and Dr. Raj Naik founded Best Value Healthcare in the Tampa area — a primary care group focused tightly on Medicare risk-bearing contracts. It was not a large organization, but it was built around the right economic model: Medicare Advantage <T>capitation</T>, with a focus on <T>risk adjustment</T> accuracy and population health management.

In November 2020, the private equity firm Arsenal Capital Partners acquired a majority stake in Best Value Healthcare. Arsenal's goal was not to own a handful of Tampa clinics. Arsenal's goal was to use Best Value as what the industry calls an "anchor platform" — the corporate MSO shell around which a much larger network could be assembled. Best Value became the legal and operational infrastructure. The acquisition spree could begin.

### The Anchor Acquisition: MAXhealth (2021)

In March 2021, Arsenal Capital used its Best Value platform to acquire MAXhealth — a larger, multi-specialty group founded in 2006 that brought 13 clinics and approximately 11,000 Medicare patients into the combined entity. The two organizations' executive teams were unified, and the consolidated network was eventually rebranded under the MaxHealth name.

This move illustrates the core mechanics of the roll-up strategy. Best Value was the acquirer because it had the MSO infrastructure, the PE backing, and the legal structure in place. MAXhealth was the target because it brought immediate scale — a large attributed patient panel, established clinic locations, and existing payer relationships. The combination created something neither could be alone: a mid-sized regional network large enough to negotiate meaningfully with health plans and to justify investment in shared operational infrastructure.

### Aggressive Scaling (2021–2025)

With the MSO infrastructure fully funded and the anchor acquisition complete, MaxHealth entered a period of aggressive expansion. Over five years, Arsenal Capital rolled up three additional larger provider organizations and thirteen smaller independent practices across Florida into the MaxHealth platform.

The pattern was consistent. Identify independent primary care practices — particularly those with strong Medicare populations and existing capitated contracts. Approach with an offer to join the MSO: the physician retains clinical autonomy and nominal ownership of their PC, but signs over the management of all non-clinical operations. In exchange, they receive a cash payment (often representing a significant multiple of the practice's annual earnings), elimination of administrative burden, and access to MaxHealth's data, technology, and care management infrastructure.

For many independent physicians — particularly those approaching retirement, struggling with the administrative load of running a practice, or simply unable to compete for MA contracts as a solo operator — this offer was highly attractive. The MSO provided what solo practice could not.

For Arsenal, each acquisition expanded the capitated patient panel, amortized the fixed cost of the MSO infrastructure across more physicians, and increased the negotiating leverage the combined entity held with health plans. Scale created value in multiple directions simultaneously.

### The Exit: PE to Payer (2026)

Having assembled a network of over 80 clinics across Florida, Arsenal Capital sold MaxHealth to Humana's CenterWell Senior Primary Care division for approximately $1 billion.

The transaction is the logical conclusion of the roll-up thesis. What Arsenal had built — a scaled, operationally integrated network of Medicare primary care practices with high RAF scores, strong quality metrics, and deep MA penetration — was precisely what a large Medicare Advantage insurer like Humana needed. CenterWell had been building its own primary care clinic network as a strategic priority, acquiring and building clinics to bring care delivery in-house rather than contracting with external providers.

<Diagram id="ch20_img03" />

For Humana, the acquisition brought 80 clinics and tens of thousands of attributed MA patients into its directly owned care delivery network overnight. That is not something a payer can replicate organically in a reasonable timeframe. For Arsenal, the sale returned a multiple on their capital that private equity targets — typically achieved by growing revenue, improving operational margins, and finding a strategic buyer who values the asset at a premium to its current earnings.

This is the classic private equity lifecycle applied to healthcare: identify a fragmented market, build the infrastructure to aggregate it, grow the asset to institutional scale, exit to a strategic acquirer.

## Why Payers Acquire These Networks

The MaxHealth-to-Humana transaction is not unusual. Across the industry, health plans have been acquiring primary care networks precisely because controlling care delivery changes their economics in fundamental ways.

A Medicare Advantage plan that contracts with an external physician group is exposed to a persistent problem: its interests and the physician group's interests are aligned in some dimensions and misaligned in others. Both want patients to receive appropriate preventive care. But the plan wants to minimize total cost of care; the physician group wants to maximize revenue. When a plan goes to renegotiate a capitation rate, the physician group has every incentive to push back.

When the plan owns the clinic, the calculation changes. Revenue the clinic earns is consolidated revenue for the enterprise. Cost the clinic generates is consolidated cost. The medical loss ratio math that governs MA plan economics — keeping claims costs below 86 cents of every premium dollar to satisfy regulatory requirements — becomes something the organization can directly manage, rather than negotiate over with an external party.

There is also a data advantage. A health plan that owns its primary care clinics has real-time access to clinical data, not just the claims feeds that typically arrive with a 60-day lag. That visibility enables earlier intervention, faster care management, and more precise risk coding.

## What This Model Means for Independent Physicians

The MSO roll-up model has materially changed the options available to independent primary care physicians — in ways that are simultaneously attractive and constraining.

The attraction is real. A small practice joining a well-run MSO platform gains access to analytics capabilities, care management staff, quality reporting infrastructure, and population health tools that would cost millions of dollars to build independently. The management fee buys operational capability that translates directly into better VBC performance. And the initial cash payment can provide financial security that decades of independent practice may not have.

The constraint is also real. A physician who has signed a long-term management services agreement with an MSO has, in most practical senses, surrendered control of the business around their clinical work. The MSO sets the administrative processes, controls the payer relationships, and owns the physical infrastructure. Exit provisions in MSAs are frequently onerous. The physician may be technically independent and clinically autonomous, but economically tethered.

Whether the trade-off is favorable depends heavily on the specific MSO, the terms of the agreement, and what the physician values most about independent practice. What is clear is that the MSO roll-up model has narrowed the path of sustainable independent primary care — particularly for physicians who want to participate meaningfully in Medicare Advantage — and has concentrated an increasing share of the operational infrastructure of US primary care in the hands of institutional investors and large payers.

---

## The Broader Pattern

MaxHealth is one data point in a much larger trend. Across the United States, private equity firms have deployed tens of billions of dollars into primary care, specialty practices, and ancillary services using the MSO structure. The market research firm Grand View Research estimated the US MSO market at over $5 billion in annual revenue and growing.

The conditions that created this opportunity — large, recurring capitated revenue streams from government payers, fragmented independent physician markets, complex administrative requirements that individual practices cannot efficiently meet, and a legal structure that enables investor ownership without violating CPOM — remain in place. The consolidation of primary care under institutional ownership is, by most measures, accelerating.

What the long-run consequences of that consolidation are for the cost, quality, and access dimensions of US healthcare is one of the central open questions in health policy. The evidence on whether PE-backed practices perform better or worse on quality and cost metrics than independent or health-system practices is genuinely mixed. What is clear is that the structure has arrived, it is operating at scale, and understanding it is increasingly prerequisite to understanding how American healthcare actually works.
