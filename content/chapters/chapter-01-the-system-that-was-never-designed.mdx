It’s your first week at a new job in healthcare.

Whatever brought you here, you sat down at your desk, opened your laptop, and got your first real look at the world you had just entered.

You went to a meeting. People spoke confidently and quickly. They talked about risk-bearing entities and <T>total cost of care</T> and quality measures and <T>capitation</T> rates. Everyone else nodded. You nodded too, because it seemed like the right thing to do.

That evening, you went home and Googled "how does the US healthcare system work." You got seventeen contradictory answers, a Wikipedia article the length of a novel, and a deep, creeping suspicion that you had made a terrible mistake.

You had not made a terrible mistake. You had simply walked into a system that was never designed to be easy to understand — because it was never designed at all.

## It Grew. It Didn't Get Built.

The US healthcare system was not built from a blueprint. It grew, layer by layer, over decades, with each new layer added to solve a specific problem that the previous layers had created or failed to address.

This is not a criticism. It is simply the reality you need to accept before anything else in this book will make sense.

The US healthcare system is not broken. It is just not a system in the designed sense. It is an ecosystem — and ecosystems have their own logic, even when that logic is hard to see.

---

## The Person and the Machine

Here is something that will help you hold the complexity of healthcare in your head.

There are always two stories happening at once.

The first story is the human one. A person gets sick. They call their doctor. They go to an appointment. They get a test, or a referral, or a prescription. They get better, or they don't, and they come back.

The second story is the machine humming behind the first one. Before that patient ever walked in the door, someone decided which doctors their insurance would cover. A contract was negotiated between a <T>health plan</T> and a physician group. A set of billing codes was assigned to every possible thing that might happen during that visit. A quality metric was defined to measure whether the right things were done. A risk score was calculated to estimate how expensive that patient was likely to be. And somewhere, a financial agreement was in place determining who would pay for the visit, how much, and under what conditions.

The patient experiences none of this. The patient just sees the doctor.

But the people who work in healthcare — who build the companies, design the contracts, write the software, manage the organizations — they live in the machine. And the machine is what this book is about.

## 4 Things That Flow

Here is the mental model that will unlock the rest of this book.

In US healthcare, four things are always flowing at once.

Care flows from providers to patients. Doctors examine people. Nurses manage medications. Specialists perform procedures. Hospitals provide intensive services. Pharmacists dispense drugs. At any given moment, millions of interactions between caregivers and patients are happening across the country. That is the care flow.

Money flows from whoever is paying to whoever is providing. And in US healthcare, the person receiving care is almost never paying the full cost directly. Instead, money moves through an elaborate chain — from the government, or from an employer, to a health plan, to a provider organization, to the physician or hospital that delivered the service. The path money takes through that chain, and who takes a cut along the way, is one of the most important things to understand about this industry.

Risk flows. This is the one that takes the most getting used to. When someone buys health insurance, they are transferring financial risk. They pay a premium upfront, and in exchange, the insurer agrees to cover their medical costs if they get sick. But the story does not end there. Insurers can transfer some of that risk to provider organizations. Provider organizations can take on responsibility for the total cost of care for a population. When a doctor's practice agrees to be accountable for how healthy their patients stay and how much their care costs, they have taken on risk. Who holds that risk — and how it gets managed — shapes almost everything about how healthcare organizations behave.

Data flows through all of it. <T>Claims</T> records. Lab results. Diagnoses. Prescriptions. Hospital admissions. Every interaction between a patient and the healthcare system generates data, and that data is used — by insurers, by provider groups, by government programs, by technology companies — to make decisions about care, about money, and about risk.

Care. Money. Risk. Data.

Four flows. That is the whole system.

Every organization you will encounter in US healthcare exists to manage one or more of these flows. Every contract, every payment model, every piece of healthcare technology is trying to direct or measure or optimize one of these flows. When you encounter something confusing — a new type of organization, a contract structure you have never seen, a business model that doesn't immediately make sense — ask yourself which of the four flows it is working with, and the confusion usually starts to lift.

## Why There Are So Many Organizations

One of the most disorienting things about healthcare, especially if you are coming from another industry, is the sheer number of different types of organizations involved in a single patient's care.

Consider a woman named Diane. She is 68 years old, lives in the suburbs, and has type 2 diabetes and high blood pressure. She sees her primary care doctor every three months. She takes four medications. She saw a cardiologist once last year. She has never thought very hard about her health insurance. She just knows she has <T>Medicare</T>.

From Diane's perspective, her healthcare experience is simple and personal. She likes her doctor. She picks up her prescriptions at the pharmacy down the street. When she has a health question, she calls the office and someone calls her back.

But behind Diane's experience, there is a long chain of organizations that most people never see. The federal government funds the program covering her care. A private insurance company manages her Medicare plan. That insurance company has contracts with a network of doctors and hospitals. Diane's doctor belongs to a physician group, which in turn belongs to a larger network, which has its own contracts with the insurance company. Someone has calculated Diane's risk score — a number that estimates how expensive she is likely to be — and her coverage has been partly priced and funded around that number. Someone is tracking whether Diane has gotten the right preventive care, because if she hasn't, the physician group may face financial consequences. Someone is managing the data that tells all of these organizations what is happening with Diane and patients like her.

Diane sees her doctor. The machine behind that visit involves at least half a dozen organizations, a web of contracts, and a stream of data that flows continuously whether or not Diane ever thinks about it.

This is why there are so many organizations in healthcare. Each one exists because there is a job to be done in managing some part of the flow of care, money, risk, or data. Once you understand what job each type of organization is doing, the complexity becomes much more manageable.

## What This Book Will Teach You

This book will take you through the US healthcare system layer by layer, in roughly the order in which the layers were added.

We will start with the most fundamental question: who pays for care? That question will introduce you to the government programs, the employers, and the health plans that sit at the top of the money flow. From there, we will follow the money down through the system, meeting the organizations that take on risk, coordinate care, and support the physicians and hospitals that actually see patients.

Along the way, we will return again and again to the four flows — care, money, risk, and data — as a framework for making sense of whatever we are looking at.

By the end, you will not have memorized every acronym or mastered every payment model. But you will be able to walk into a meeting, hear people talking about healthcare in a language that once felt completely foreign, and understand not just the words but the logic underneath them.

The system that was never designed still has a logic to it. Finding that logic is what this book is for.

In the next chapter, we will begin at the top of the money flow — with the question of who, in the United States, actually pays for healthcare.
