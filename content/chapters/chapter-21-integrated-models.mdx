Every organization in Part VI has been moving toward integration — combining insurance, care delivery, data, and infrastructure under unified ownership. Two organizations have taken this further, and earlier, than anyone else.

<T>Kaiser Permanente</T> built a fully integrated system from scratch in the 1940s. <T>UnitedHealth Group</T> assembled one through four decades of acquisition and expansion. Both represent the same destination — control over the entire healthcare stack — reached by entirely different paths.

---

## Kaiser Permanente: Built From the Ground Up

Kaiser Permanente began in the 1940s as prepaid healthcare for industrial workers. Henry Kaiser needed a way to keep tens of thousands of construction and shipyard workers healthy in remote locations. Physician Sidney Garfield built a system where workers paid a fixed monthly fee and the medical team was responsible for keeping them well. The model worked, opened to the public after World War II, and became the template for what we now call <T>value-based care</T> — decades before anyone used that phrase.

Today Kaiser serves roughly 12 million members across eight US regions. Its structure has three interlocking parts.

Kaiser Foundation <T>Health Plan</T> is the insurer — it collects premiums, holds insurance risk, and manages benefits. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals is the owned hospital system — surgery, inpatient, emergency. The Permanente Medical Groups are the physician partnerships — legally independent from Kaiser but contracted exclusively to it, a structure designed to preserve physician autonomy while maintaining clinical integration.

What makes Kaiser genuinely different is its data infrastructure. KP HealthConnect, its enterprise-wide <T>EHR</T> built on Epic, is shared across every care setting in the system. A patient's complete record — every visit, medication, lab result, hospitalization — is visible to every Kaiser clinician who touches their care. No fax machines. No duplicate tests. No lost discharge summaries. The data integration is the clinical integration.

The limitation is geographic. Building a Kaiser requires owning hospitals, practices, and insurance in the same market — a massive capital commitment. Kaiser has grown by deepening its presence in existing regions, not by replicating itself nationally.

## UnitedHealth Group: Integration by Acquisition

UnitedHealth Group started as a conventional insurer in 1977 and spent the next four decades acquiring every adjacent business it could identify. The result is the largest healthcare company in the world by revenue, with two major divisions.

UnitedHealthcare is the insurance division — commercial employer plans, the largest <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> enrollment of any US insurer, and <T>Medicaid</T> managed care across dozens of states.

<T>Optum</T> is the health services division, organized into three segments. OptumHealth is the care delivery arm — through a series of major acquisitions, it now employs or closely partners with over 90,000 physicians, making UnitedHealth Group one of the largest physician employers in the US. It operates clinics, surgical centers, behavioral health programs, home-based care, and Optum Care, a VBC management platform. OptumRx is one of the three largest Pharmacy Benefit Managers in the country. OptumInsight provides data, analytics, and technology services to health plans, hospitals, and government agencies — including many that compete with UnitedHealthcare.

In many markets, a UnitedHealthcare MA member is now insured by UnitedHealthcare, cared for by an Optum physician, and has their prescriptions managed by OptumRx — all within the same parent company.

## The Logic — and the Concerns

The strategic logic is clear: controlling more of the healthcare value chain means better information, better cost management, and — in theory — better care coordination. Kaiser's single EHR eliminates the data silos that cause so much harm in fragmented care. Optum's scale gives UHG negotiating leverage at every layer.

<Diagram id="ch21_img03" />

But integration at this scale raises serious concerns. When an insurer and a care delivery organization serve the same member under the same parent company, the financial interests of the insurer — lower utilization, lower cost — can conflict with the clinical interests of the patient. Whether Optum-affiliated physicians face pressure to order fewer tests, make fewer referrals, or shorten hospital stays because their parent company profits from it is a live regulatory question.

Market power is a second concern. A company that owns payer, provider, pharmacy, and analytics simultaneously has leverage over every independent competitor at every layer. The Department of Justice has scrutinized UHG's acquisitions; antitrust questions about the combined entity's market influence are ongoing.

Kaiser raises different concerns: its geographic limitation means it largely serves urban, relatively affluent populations — not always those who most need integrated <T>care management</T>.

## What These Models Tell Us

The structural direction of US healthcare is toward integration. The most influential organizations are moving to control more of the care journey, not less. Whether that produces genuinely better care — more coordinated, more equitable, more efficient — or primarily consolidates market power remains one of the genuinely open questions in healthcare policy.

What is clear is that these organizations are not interesting case studies at the edge of the industry. They are its center of gravity. Understanding how they are structured and where they are moving is essential for anyone who works within the system they are reshaping.
