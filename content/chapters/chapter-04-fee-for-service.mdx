Imagine you take your car to a mechanic. They look it over, replace the brake pads, change the oil, and fix a slow leak in one of the tires. When you pick it up, you get a bill that lists every item: brake pads, labor, oil filter, oil, tire repair. You pay for each thing that was done.

That is, in essence, how <T>fee-for-service</T> medicine works.

A patient comes in. The doctor examines them, orders a lab test, and prescribes a medication. Each of those things — the exam, the test, the prescription — is a billable service. Each one generates a charge. The charges get submitted to whoever is paying. The payer reimburses the provider. The provider gets paid and moves on to the next patient.

Fee-for-service (<T>FFS</T>)  is still the dominant payment model in American healthcare. Understanding how it works is essential, because everything that comes after it (managed care, <T>value-based care</T>, <T>capitation</T>) was designed specifically as a response to its limitations.

---

## The Language of Billing: CPT Codes

For any of this to work, there needs to be a shared language — a way for every doctor in the country to describe what they did in a form that every insurance company can read and price.

That language is the <T>CPT code</T> — which stands for Current Procedural Terminology. <T>CPT codes</T> are a standardized set of five-digit numbers maintained by the American Medical Association, where each code corresponds to a specific medical service or procedure.

A routine office visit for an established patient might be billed as 99213 or 99214, depending on complexity. A knee replacement is a different code. A colonoscopy is a different code. A 20-minute psychotherapy session is a different code. There are thousands of them, covering nearly every interaction between a patient and a provider.

When a doctor sees a patient, someone in the practice — often a medical coder or the physician themselves — reviews the documentation and assigns the appropriate <T>CPT code</T> or codes for what happened. They also assign an <T>ICD-10 code</T>, which describes the diagnosis — why the patient was there in the first place. The visit was for a type 2 diabetes checkup. The procedure was to remove a lesion. The reason for the imaging was back pain of more than 12 weeks' duration.

Together, the <T>CPT codes</T> (what was done) and the <T>ICD-10</T> codes (why it was done) form the core of a medical claim.

---

## From Visit to Payment

Once the codes are assigned, the claim begins its journey.

The practice submits the claim — usually electronically — to the patient's payer. That might be a commercial <T>health plan</T>, <T>Medicare</T>, <T>Medicaid</T>, or a self-insured employer's administrator. The payer receives the claim and runs it through a process called adjudication: checking whether the service is covered under the patient's plan, whether the provider is in-network, whether <T>prior authorization</T> was obtained if required, and what the contracted rate is for that specific service.

If everything checks out, the payer issues a payment — not necessarily the full amount billed, but the contracted rate that the provider and payer negotiated in advance. That contracted rate is almost always lower than what was billed. The provider accepts it as payment in full (minus whatever the patient owes in copays or deductibles), and the transaction is complete.

This cycle — visit, code, claim, adjudicate, pay — happens hundreds of millions of times a year across the US healthcare system. It is the basic plumbing of how money moves from payers to providers.

## The Problem Built Into the Model

Fee-for-service has real virtues. It is straightforward. Providers know how they will be paid. The more patients they see, the more revenue they generate. For a new or growing practice, that predictability matters.

But the incentive structure it creates is, at best, neutral toward good outcomes — and at worst, actively works against them.

Under <T>fee-for-service</T>, a provider is paid for doing things. Not for results. Not for keeping patients healthy. Not for preventing the hospital admission that might have been avoided with better follow-up care. If a patient with poorly controlled diabetes ends up in the emergency room, the hospital gets paid for the ER visit. If that same patient had received better outpatient management, the ER visit never happens — and the hospital collects nothing.

This is not a criticism of individual doctors, most of whom went into medicine to help people, not to run up bills. It is a structural observation about what the model rewards. Volume is visible and billable. Prevention is invisible and, under <T>fee-for-service</T>, financially unrewarded.

There is a second problem: fragmentation. Because every service is billed separately, no one in the <T>fee-for-service</T> system is financially responsible for the patient's overall care. The primary care doctor is paid for the office visit. The specialist is paid for the consultation. The hospital is paid for the admission. Nobody has an economic stake in how these pieces fit together — or whether the patient falls through the gaps between them.

## Why It Still Dominates

Given these problems, why does <T>fee-for-service</T> still account for the majority of healthcare payments in the United States?

Partly because it is familiar and deeply embedded. The billing infrastructure — the codes, the <T>claims</T> systems, the adjudication technology — has been built up over decades and represents an enormous investment. Changing it is not simple.

Partly because the alternatives are harder. As we will see in the next chapter, <T>value-based care</T> models that try to pay for outcomes rather than volume require data, <T>attribution</T>, measurement, and coordination that <T>fee-for-service</T> does not demand.

And partly because, for many types of care, <T>fee-for-service</T> works fine. A patient walks in with a broken wrist. The doctor sets it and bills for the visit. The model fits. The problems emerge most clearly in the management of complex, chronic, ongoing conditions — the kind of care that is increasingly central to healthcare spending as the population ages.

Fee-for-service is not going away. But over the past two decades, the healthcare system has been building a new layer on top of it — one designed to change the incentives and hold providers accountable for something more than just showing up and billing.
