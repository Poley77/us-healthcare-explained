The Economics and Incentives of US Healthcare

Understanding how the healthcare system works is one thing. Understanding why every actor in it behaves the way they do is another — and it is the difference between being informed and being fluent.

Every organization in US healthcare is responding to a specific set of financial incentives, risk exposures, and performance pressures. When you understand those incentives for each stakeholder, behavior that otherwise seems irrational, bureaucratic, or inexplicable becomes entirely predictable. The prior authorization that frustrates a patient. The readmission penalty that changes a hospital's discharge planning. The RAF coding program that drives thousands of physician chart reviews each year. The <T>benchmark</T> dispute that paralyzes an ACO's board. All of it makes sense once you know what each party is optimizing for.

This chapter applies the same analytical framework to every major stakeholder in the ecosystem. For each one, we ask six questions: What are they? How do they make money? What risk do they bear? What metrics matter? Who do they contract with? And what keeps them up at night?

## CMS

What it is: The <T>Centers for <T>Medicare</T> & <T>Medicaid</T> Services</T> is the federal agency that administers Medicare and sets the federal rules for Medicaid. It is the single largest payer in US healthcare — spending over $1.5 trillion annually — and the most powerful regulatory force shaping how care is organized and paid for.

How it makes money: CMS is not a profit-seeking entity. It is funded by a combination of payroll taxes, general tax revenue, and beneficiary premiums. Its goal is not profit but stewardship: ensuring the programs it administers are delivering value, meeting quality standards, and remaining financially sustainable for future generations.

What risk it bears: The primary risk CMS faces is program solvency — particularly the depletion of the Medicare Hospital Insurance Trust Fund, which funds Part A. Actuaries have been projecting the potential insolvency of the trust fund for decades; every administration faces pressure to manage spending without reducing care quality. CMS also bears policy risk: changes in political priorities can shift program rules, payment rates, and the appetite for <T>value-based care</T> initiatives.

What metrics matter: CMS tracks per-capita Medicare spending, the MSSP savings rate, the distribution of MA Stars ratings across plans, and the percentage of Medicare payments flowing through alternative payment models. Its stated goal has been to move a significant majority of Medicare spending into value-based arrangements, though progress has been slower than originally projected.

Who it contracts with: CMS holds plan contracts with MA insurers, participation agreements with MSSP and <T>ACO REACH</T> ACOs, and conditions of participation with every hospital and provider receiving Medicare payment.

What keeps it up at night: MA overcoding — the risk that plans and their partners are systematically inflating risk scores to capture higher payments — is perhaps the most acute concern. The Office of Inspector General estimates billions of dollars in improper MA payments annually. Political pressure on MA payment rates is perennial: any reduction in the benchmark threatens plan participation and member benefits. And the long-term fiscal trajectory of Medicare remains a structural concern that no single administration can resolve.

## Health Plans (Commercial)

What they are: Commercial health plans — UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, Cigna, Elevance Health, BCBS affiliates — collect premiums from employers and individuals and agree to cover the cost of members' healthcare in exchange. Their core business is managing the spread between premium collected and medical costs paid.

How they make money: The fundamental economics are: premiums minus medical costs minus administrative costs equals underwriting profit. The <T>Medical Loss Ratio</T> (MLR) — the percentage of premium dollars spent on medical care — is the central metric. The ACA mandates minimum MLRs of 80% (individual/small group) and 85% (large group), capping administrative and profit margins. Additional revenue comes from <T>Administrative Services Only</T> (ASO) fees charged to self-insured employers, investment income on reserves, and increasingly from VBC arrangements that allow plans to earn more when care is managed efficiently.

What risk they bear: Medical cost overrun is the existential risk — if a bad flu season, a new high-cost drug, or a shift in utilization pushes costs above premium, plans absorb the loss. Stars degradation threatens MA bonus payments. Network adequacy failures can trigger regulatory action. And in a competitive market, over-pricing relative to competitors drives membership loss.

What metrics matter: MLR is watched obsessively. <T>Stars rating</T> determines bonus eligibility. Membership growth signals competitive health. Administrative cost ratio — keeping back-office costs low — determines margin sustainability.

Who they contract with: Employers (ASO or fully-insured), providers (network participation agreements), ACOs (VBC contracts), and PBMs for pharmacy benefit administration.

What keeps them up at night: Medical trend outpacing premium increases is the chronic worry. A Stars rating dropping below 4 can cost a large MA plan hundreds of millions in lost bonuses. Regulatory scrutiny of prior authorization practices and <T>claims</T> denial rates has intensified. And the growing power of large health systems — which can walk away from network negotiations or charge very high rates — compresses plan margins.

## <T>Medicare Advantage</T> Plans

What they are: MA plans are private health insurers licensed by CMS to offer Medicare coverage as an alternative to <T>Traditional Medicare</T>. They receive a risk-adjusted monthly payment from CMS per enrolled member and are responsible for delivering (or arranging) all Medicare-covered benefits within that budget.

How they make money: CMS pays each MA plan a benchmark PMPM, adjusted by each member's <T>RAF score</T>. The plan's margin is the difference between the total <T>capitation</T> received and the <T>total cost of care</T> delivered. Plans that bid below the benchmark receive a rebate they must invest in supplemental benefits — which is why MA plans can offer dental, vision, and gym memberships that traditional Medicare does not. Stars bonuses of approximately 5% additional capitation for plans rated 4 stars or above add a meaningful revenue layer.

What risk they bear: MA plans bear the full insurance risk — if their members are sicker than the RAF score predicts, or if medical costs rise faster than anticipated, the plan absorbs the loss. RAF coding accuracy is a major financial exposure: a CMS audit that recaptures overcoded payments can represent billions in repayment obligations. Stars falling below 4 triggers both revenue loss (no bonus) and market damage (members tend to choose higher-rated plans).

What metrics matter: Stars rating is supreme. Medical Loss Ratio determines profitability. RAF score per member determines revenue. Inpatient admission rate per thousand members is the most closely watched utilization metric — unnecessary hospitalizations are the single largest driver of MA cost overruns.

Who they contract with: CMS (plan contract), provider groups and ACOs (VBC sub-contracts), MSOs (<T>care management</T> and analytics services), and PBMs.

What keeps them up at night: CMS rate adjustments — the benchmark rate is reset annually, and cuts can immediately compress margins for plans that have built business plans around prior rates. RAF audits and repayment demands. An unexpectedly sick membership cohort (adverse selection), particularly after a large coverage expansion. And Stars degradation, which in large plans represents a step-function revenue change.

## ACOs

What they are: Accountable Care Organizations are provider-led or jointly led entities that accept contractual accountability for the quality and total cost of care for a defined attributed patient population. They participate in <T>shared savings</T> programs (primarily MSSP and ACO REACH) and increasingly in direct MA value-based contracts.

How they make money: ACOs earn a share — typically 50–75% — of net savings generated against a CMS-established benchmark, subject to meeting minimum quality thresholds. Some ACOs also earn <T>sub-capitation</T> PMPM payments from MA plans, and many receive care management fees from payers or through CMS billing codes. The financial model is inherently retrospective: savings are calculated 9–15 months after the performance year ends.

What risk they bear: ACOs that have accepted <T>downside risk</T> tracks owe money back to CMS if actual spending exceeds the benchmark. Even upside-only ACOs bear the risk of investing heavily in care management infrastructure that generates no return if savings fall below the <T>Minimum Savings Rate</T>. Quality gate failure — not meeting minimum quality thresholds — blocks access to any savings payment regardless of cost performance.

What metrics matter: Actual versus benchmark spending is the primary financial metric. <T>HEDIS</T> quality scores — particularly chronic disease management, preventive care, and patient experience — determine whether the quality gate is cleared. Avoidable admission rates and 30-day readmission rates are the most actionable utilization metrics. And the 12-month reconciliation lag means cash flow management is a constant operational concern.

Who they contract with: CMS (MSSP or ACO REACH participation agreement), MA plans (VBC contract), IPAs (sub-contracting), and MSOs (operational and technology services).

What keeps them up at night: A benchmark set too low — perhaps because the ACO already drove down costs in a prior year — makes earning savings nearly impossible. Quality gate failure can wipe out a year of financial performance in a single ruling. Data lag means ACOs may not know they are trending over benchmark until it is too late to intervene. And the reconciliation wait creates cash flow pressure for organizations that have spent real money on care management all year without receiving the savings payment.

## IPAs

What they are: Independent Practice Associations are legal entities that aggregate independent physicians into a single contracting unit — enabling them to negotiate with health plans and ACOs collectively rather than individually.

How they make money: IPAs receive sub-capitation PMPM payments from ACOs or health plans and distribute the net of those payments to member physicians after retaining an administrative margin. In shared savings arrangements, the IPA receives a portion of the ACO's shared savings distribution and passes it through to member practices based on performance. Some IPAs charge membership fees or administrative fees to participating physicians.

What risk they bear: IPAs often bear pass-through risk from the ACO layer above — if the ACO accepts downside risk, that exposure can flow down to the IPA and its members. High-cost outlier patients — a member whose care costs far exceed the capitation allocated — are a recurrent threat in smaller IPAs without <T>stop-loss</T> coverage. And if member physicians disengage from care management programs, the IPA's performance suffers without the IPA having direct control.

What metrics matter: Adequacy of the sub-capitation PMPM relative to the actual cost of caring for attributed patients is the fundamental financial question. Member physician quality scores feed into the ACO's quality gate. <T>Attribution</T> retention — keeping attributed patients associated with IPA physicians — determines revenue stability. And the amount of savings actually distributed to member physicians determines whether IPA participation is economically attractive.

Who they contract with: ACOs (participation agreement), health plans (direct VBC contracts in some markets), member physicians, and MSOs for administrative services.

What keeps them up at night: PMPM inadequacy — particularly when carve-outs mean the IPA is responsible for costs it didn't anticipate. High-cost outlier patients without stop-loss protection. Physician disengagement, which reduces quality performance and shared savings eligibility without being directly correctable by the IPA. And attribution disputes — patients who have been cared for by IPA physicians but are attributed elsewhere — reducing the revenue base.

## MSOs

What they are: Management Services Organizations provide administrative, operational, and technology services to physician practices, IPAs, and ACOs under a <T>Management Services Agreement</T> (MSA). They do not deliver clinical care and generally do not hold clinical risk.

How they make money: The core MSO revenue model is a management fee — typically 15–25% of the physician practice's revenue, or a flat monthly fee per physician. Some MSOs also earn performance-based fees tied to quality or shared savings outcomes of the practices they support. Larger, investor-backed MSOs may hold equity in the practices they support or take a share of the VBC economics as part of the joint venture or contractual structure.

What risk they bear: MSOs bear no direct clinical or insurance risk. Their exposure is primarily reputational and commercial: if the practices they support perform poorly in VBC arrangements, those practices may exit the MSO relationship, reducing the MSO's revenue. Indirectly, MSOs that support practices in downside risk tracks may face pressure if <T>clawback</T> demands flow to the practices they are contractually serving.

What metrics matter: Revenue per physician under management drives the top line. Cost-to-serve ratio determines whether the management fee exceeds the cost of delivering the services. Quality measure performance at supported practices determines whether the MSO's platform is genuinely adding value. And practice retention — keeping practices in the MSO relationship long-term — is critical to the business model's economics.

Who they contract with: Physician practices (MSA), ACOs and IPAs (operational support contracts), health plans (direct relationships in some models), and technology vendors.

What keeps them up at night: Practice churn — a physician group that builds internal capabilities and exits the MSO relationship takes revenue with it. Platform commoditization as analytics and care management tools become more widely available. And the knock-on effect of clawback demands on the practices they support — a financially stressed practice cannot afford its MSO fee.

## Hospitals and Health Systems

What they are: Hospitals are the highest-cost care delivery setting in US healthcare. Health systems are organized networks of hospitals, physician practices, ambulatory care facilities, and supporting services, often operating across multiple markets. They range from small community hospitals to massive academic medical centers and regional systems with tens of billions in annual revenue.

How they make money: The majority of hospital revenue flows from FFS claims paid according to Diagnosis Related Groups (DRGs) — CMS's system of <T>bundled payments</T> for inpatient cases, which pays a fixed amount per admission based on the diagnosis and procedure category. Commercial plans negotiate rates as a multiple of Medicare — often 150–300% of Medicare rates for large systems with market leverage. Additional revenue comes from outpatient services, employed physician groups, and increasingly from bundled payment programs and VBC arrangements.

What risk they bear: Uncompensated care — treating patients who cannot pay — is an ongoing margin drain for hospitals, particularly those serving high-Medicaid or uninsured populations. Payer mix shifts as more patients move from commercial coverage (high rates) to Medicare or Medicaid (lower rates) compress margins. CMS imposes Hospital-Acquired Condition (HAC) penalties and readmission penalties that reduce payment for hospitals with high rates of preventable complications or avoidable readmissions. And the long-term trend of VBC shifting care to lower-cost settings — outpatient, home — directly threatens the inpatient volume that hospitals depend on.

What metrics matter: Case Mix Index (CMI) measures the average clinical complexity of inpatient cases — higher CMI means higher DRG payments. Readmission rates drive penalty exposure. Length of stay affects cost efficiency. Payer mix determines effective revenue per case. And operating margin — which is thin for most hospitals, averaging 1–3% in good years — determines financial sustainability.

Who they contract with: Health plans (network participation and rate contracts), employed physicians, ACOs and VBC entities as part of broader provider networks, and CMS (conditions of participation).

What keeps them up at night: Margin compression is the existential concern — labor costs, supply chain inflation, and lower reimbursement rates are squeezing the thin margins that most hospitals operate on. VBC's systematic effort to reduce avoidable hospitalizations directly attacks inpatient volume. Readmission penalty exposure creates operational pressure. And the growing negotiating leverage of large commercial health plans — or the entry of vertically integrated players like <T>Optum</T> that can steer patients away from independent hospitals — threatens market position.

## Primary Care Providers

What they are: Primary care physicians — family medicine, internal medicine, pediatrics, geriatrics — are the first point of contact for most patients in the healthcare system. In the VBC context, they are the most strategically important providers: the quality of primary care management is the single largest driver of whether a patient avoids unnecessary hospitalizations, emergency visits, and specialist overuse.

How they make money: Most primary care practices still generate the majority of their revenue from FFS claims — Evaluation and Management (E&M) codes billed for office visits and procedures. In VBC arrangements, this FFS baseline is supplemented by sub-capitation PMPMs for attributed MA or ACO patients, care management fees billed under CCM/TCM billing codes, quality bonuses tied to HEDIS measure performance, and an annual shared savings distribution if the ACO or IPA performs well. In mature VBC practices, these additional streams can represent 20–40% of total revenue.

What risk they bear: Under capitated arrangements, the primary care practice bears the financial risk of spending more on care management than the PMPM covers — particularly if the attributed panel contains more complex patients than anticipated. Clawback demands from retrospective audits are a genuine fear. In downside risk tracks, the practice may owe money back if total attributed population costs exceed the benchmark. And the administrative burden of VBC — <T>care gap</T> tracking, quality reporting, data integration — represents a real cost in physician time.

What metrics matter: Panel size determines revenue scale. Visits per day drives FFS revenue. HEDIS care gap closure rates determine quality bonus eligibility and ACO performance. <T>HCC</T> capture rate — the completeness with which patient diagnoses are documented and coded — directly affects RAF scores and the PMPM the practice receives. And increasingly, total cost of care for the attributed panel is the ultimate accountability metric.

Who they contract with: Health plans (provider participation agreements), IPAs (membership agreements), MSOs (management services agreements), and ACOs (participation agreements). A single primary care physician may simultaneously be party to four or five contracts across these relationships.

What keeps them up at night: The administrative burden of VBC is a constant complaint — quality reporting, data integration, care gap tracking, and care management require time and staff that small practices cannot always afford. Capitation with an unexpectedly sick panel can turn a profitable arrangement into a financial loss. Clawback demands — retroactive recovery of prior payments — are deeply feared. And the fundamental tension between the <T>pace</T> of a full clinical schedule and the longitudinal <T>population health</T> work that VBC requires remains unresolved for most primary care physicians in the system.

## The Pattern Across All Stakeholders

Reading across all eight stakeholders, a consistent pattern emerges.

Every organization is managing a tension between short-term revenue and long-term performance. FFS rewards volume now; VBC rewards population health over time. The organizations that navigate this successfully are those that can fund the transition — investing in care management, data infrastructure, and quality improvement — while maintaining the FFS revenue they depend on in the short term.

Data is the lever that every stakeholder is trying to pull. CMS wants better data on where its money is going. Plans want better data on their members' risk. ACOs want real-time data on utilization trends. MSOs are built around data. Hospitals want data on readmission risk. Physicians want data on their attributed population. The organizations that win in modern healthcare are those that can turn data into action faster than their competitors.

The incentive misalignment between layers is real and consequential. What CMS wants — lower spending, higher quality — is not always what a <T>health plan</T> wants, or an ACO, or a hospital. The genius of well-designed VBC contracts is that they create partial alignment: everyone earns more when care is managed better. The failure mode of poorly designed contracts is that they create perverse incentives at each layer — cutting costs in ways that harm patients, gaming quality metrics, or simply maintaining the status quo while collecting VBC fees.

The physician is the hinge. In the end, every contract, every payment model, every organization in this ecosystem is trying to influence what happens in the exam room. The physician's clinical judgment — what tests to order, what medications to prescribe, when to refer, how to counsel a patient about their lifestyle — is the primary driver of healthcare cost and quality. Everything else in this ecosystem is infrastructure designed to support, incentivize, measure, and sometimes constrain that judgment.

Understanding the economics and incentives of US healthcare does not make the system less complex. But it makes the complexity comprehensible. And comprehensibility is the beginning of the ability to change things.
