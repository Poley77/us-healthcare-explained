In everyday life, risk means the chance something bad might happen. In healthcare finance, it means something more specific: who is responsible when care costs more than expected?

Under <T>fee-for-service</T>, the answer is simple — the payer. The insurer agreed to cover the patient, so the insurer pays the bills. If the patient turns out to be sicker than expected and generates more <T>claims</T>, that's the insurer's problem. The provider just submits claims and gets paid.

<T>Value-based care</T> changes this. It moves some — or all — of that financial responsibility from the payer onto the provider organization. The more risk a provider takes on, the more they stand to gain if they manage care well, and the more they stand to lose if they don't.

This is the <T>risk spectrum</T>, and understanding it is essential to making sense of the entire VBC ecosystem.

---

## The Four Levels

Fee-for-service sits at the bottom of the risk spectrum. The provider does the work, submits a claim, and gets paid. No exposure, no upside beyond the fee itself.

<T>Shared savings</T> (upside only) is the entry point into value-based care for most provider organizations. At the start of a contract period, the payer and provider agree on a spending <T>benchmark</T> — what the payer would expect to spend on a defined population under normal circumstances. If the provider manages care well and actual spending comes in below that benchmark, they receive a share of the savings as a bonus. If spending runs over, nothing bad happens — the provider simply doesn't earn the bonus. All the <T>downside risk</T> stays with the payer.

This is called upside-only because there is potential upside — the bonus — but no downside. It is often a provider organization's first step into VBC because it creates an incentive to manage care better without threatening financial survival.

Shared risk (two-sided) raises the stakes. Now the arrangement runs in both directions. The provider shares in savings if costs come in below target — but if costs exceed the benchmark, the provider owes money back to the payer. This is also called downside risk. Taking on downside risk requires operational confidence: robust data, <T>care management</T> programs, and the organizational capacity to actually influence cost outcomes. A provider that accepts downside risk without those capabilities is taking a significant financial gamble.

Full <T>capitation</T> is the most complete transfer of risk. The payer gives the provider a fixed monthly payment — called a per-member per-month or PMPM rate — for each patient assigned to them. The provider must deliver (or arrange) all necessary care within that budget. If a patient is expensive, the provider absorbs the cost. If the patient is healthy and the care is well managed, the provider keeps the surplus. The payer has effectively converted a variable cost into a fixed one. The provider has become, in financial terms, something closer to an insurer.

## Risk and Reward Are the Same Lever

A crucial thing to understand about the risk spectrum is that risk and reward are not separate concepts — they are the same lever.

More financial risk means more potential financial reward. A provider in a pure FFS arrangement cannot earn more than their contracted rates, no matter how well they manage care. A provider in a capitated arrangement that runs an efficient, high-quality care model can generate significant margin. The same is true in reverse: a capitated provider facing an unexpectedly sick population, or one that fails to coordinate care effectively, can lose money quickly.

This is why the question of how much risk to take on is one of the most consequential strategic decisions a provider organization makes. Too little risk and there is no incentive to change. Too much risk before the organization has the capabilities to manage it, and the financial exposure can be severe.

---

## Why It Matters

The risk level a provider operates under shapes almost everything about how they run their organization — what data they track, what staff they hire, what technology they invest in, how they think about patients between visits.

A fee-for-service practice thinks in appointments. A capitated practice thinks in populations.

That difference is at the heart of why the VBC ecosystem exists — and why the organizations we will meet in the coming chapters (ACOs, IPAs, MSOs) were built.
