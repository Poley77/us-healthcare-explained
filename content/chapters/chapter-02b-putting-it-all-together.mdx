Before you can understand anything else about the US healthcare system, you need to know what actually happens once someone needs care — and how the money moves behind the scenes.

By now you've met the major funding sources. But understanding *who* pays is only half the story. This chapter maps the entire landscape: the four players, who ends up in which programme, what they actually pay, and why the same insurance policy can cost one person nothing and another person $600 a month.

---

## The Four Players

Almost every dollar spent in US healthcare flows between four groups.

<Diagram id="ch02_img05" />

Each participant has different incentives. Patients want the best care possible. Providers want to deliver high-quality treatment while being fairly compensated. Payers aim to control costs without compromising outcomes, while producers invest in developing new medicines and technologies and seek a return on those investments. Much of the complexity of the US healthcare system comes from balancing these competing priorities.

---

## Under 65: Where You Start Depends on Your Employer

For working-age Americans, the first question is simple: does your employer offer health insurance?

If it does, that is almost always your best option. Employers are legally required to pay roughly 70–80% of your monthly premium — a subsidy no marketplace plan can match. The trade-off is limited choice: your HR department selects two to four plans and that is your entire menu. Typically you will see an HMO option (cheaper premium, restricted network), a PPO option (more expensive, see almost any doctor), and sometimes an HDHP with a Health Savings Account (low premium, high deductible — favoured by healthy employees who want to build tax-free savings).

Premiums are also deducted pre-tax directly from your paycheck, which saves you income tax on top of the employer subsidy.

If you are self-employed, between jobs, or your employer does not offer coverage, you move to the next question: how much do you earn?

---

## Under 65, No Employer: Income Determines Your Path

For Americans without employer coverage, your annual household income determines whether you qualify for <T>Medicaid</T> or must buy a plan through the <T>ACA</T> Marketplace.

<Diagram id="ch02_img10" />

In the 41 states that have expanded <T>Medicaid</T> under the Affordable Care Act, a single adult earning below roughly $22,025 per year (138% of the Federal Poverty Level) qualifies for <T>Medicaid</T> — state-run coverage that is free or near-free, with minimal copays. Above that line, you buy a plan on the ACA Marketplace instead.

The system is designed to hand off seamlessly. If you go to Healthcare.gov and enter your income, the site automatically determines which programme you fall into and routes you accordingly. You do not have to figure it out yourself.

One important note: ten states have *not* expanded <T>Medicaid</T>. In states like Texas and Florida, the income thresholds are far stricter, and many low-income adults fall into a coverage gap — earning too much for the restricted state <T>Medicaid</T> but not enough to receive meaningful Marketplace subsidies.

## How the ACA Marketplace Works: Same Plan, Very Different Price

The ACA Marketplace is not one-size-fits-all. The government subsidises both the monthly premium and, for lower-income enrollees, the actual benefits inside the plan. So the same insurance policy can look completely different depending on what you earn.

<Diagram id="ch02_img11" />

There are two separate subsidies at work. The first is a **Premium Tax Credit** — the government pays a chunk of your monthly premium directly to your insurer, scaling down as your income rises until the credit disappears entirely above roughly $63,840 per year for a single adult.

The second, less understood subsidy is **Cost-Sharing Reductions (CSRs)**. These only apply if you earn below roughly $39,900 per year *and* you choose a Silver-tier plan. CSRs physically alter the insurance policy — lowering your deductible from $5,000+ down to as little as $500, and shrinking your doctor visit copays to $5–$15. A low earner on a Silver plan can end up with near-Platinum-level benefits for almost nothing per month.

The key point: lower-income Americans do not get worse insurance. They get the same doctors, the same hospitals, and the same covered conditions. The government simply pays a larger share of both the bill and the care.

**For healthy high earners**, the calculus flips. Because they receive no subsidies and face full-price deductibles on Silver plans, many choose a Bronze High-Deductible Health Plan paired with a Health Savings Account (HSA). This keeps monthly premiums as low as possible while letting them deposit pre-tax money into a savings account that rolls over year after year and can be invested tax-free — building a cushion for any future medical costs.

## Employer vs. Marketplace: The Key Differences

<Diagram id="ch02_img12" />

The structural difference matters enormously: if your employer offers a plan the government considers "affordable" (costing less than roughly 9% of your household income), you lose all rights to Marketplace subsidies. Buying a Marketplace plan instead will almost always cost you significantly more. The right financial move, almost without exception, is to take what your employer offers.

## At 65: You Enter Medicare

At 65, nearly all Americans transition onto <T>Medicare</T> — the federal insurance programme run by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services. Your employment history, and any taxes you paid while working, now determine exactly what you owe.

<Diagram id="ch02_img13" />

**Part A (Hospital)** covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facilities, and hospice care. If you or your spouse paid Medicare payroll taxes for at least ten years, your monthly Part A premium is $0. You already paid for it throughout your working life. If you did not accumulate that work history — recent immigrants, or those who worked entirely in cash economies — you can pay up to $505 per month.

**Part B (Medical)** covers doctor visits, outpatient procedures, and preventive care. Unlike Part A, nobody receives Part B for free. The standard premium is $202.90 per month in 2026, and it is automatically deducted from your monthly Social Security cheque. Higher earners pay more through a surcharge called IRMAA.

After you meet your annual Part B deductible ($283), the government pays 80% of every approved bill and you pay the remaining 20%. This sounds manageable until you consider that Original Medicare has **no annual out-of-pocket maximum**. A $100,000 cancer treatment leaves you owing $20,000 with no ceiling — which is why almost no one relies on Original Medicare alone.

## The Three Safety Nets

Because the uncapped 20% coinsurance is a genuine financial risk, roughly 90% of Medicare beneficiaries add one of three protections on top of Original Medicare.

<Diagram id="ch02_img14" />

**Medigap** is a private supplemental policy that steps in and pays the 20% coinsurance and most deductibles on your behalf. Your monthly medical bills drop to near zero — but you pay an extra $150–$300+ per month in Medigap premiums. The major advantage is total doctor freedom: you can see any provider in the US who accepts Medicare, nationwide.

**Medicare Advantage (Part C)** takes a different approach. Instead of supplementing Original Medicare, it replaces it entirely with a private plan. The government hands the money it would have spent on you directly to a private insurer, which then manages your care. Most plans charge $0 in additional monthly premium (beyond the Part B fee you still owe the government), but you pay flat copays each time you see a doctor, and you must stay within a local network. The key protection is a legal annual out-of-pocket cap — no matter what happens to your health, your exposure is capped, typically around $9,250.

**Dual eligibility** (Medicare + Medicaid) is available to low-income seniors who qualify for both simultaneously. Medicaid pays Medicare's deductibles, copays, and premiums, making coverage effectively free. About one in five Medicare beneficiaries qualifies.

## The Medicare Alphabet Explained

<T>Medicare</T>'s Part A, B, C, and D labelling confuses almost everyone. The simplest way to understand it is as two completely separate pathways.

<Diagram id="ch02_img15" />

Under **Pathway 1 (Original Medicare)**, you keep Parts A and B — the government-run programmes — and add a standalone Part D plan for prescription drugs. If you want to cover the 20% gap, you add an optional Medigap policy. You manage three separate policies, but you have complete freedom to see any Medicare-accepting provider in the country.

Under **Pathway 2 (Medicare Advantage, Part C)**, a private insurer bundles everything together. Part A, Part B, and usually Part D are all wrapped into a single plan. You deal with one card, one network, and usually one lower (or zero) extra premium. The trade-off is that you must stay within your plan's local doctor network.

One constant applies regardless of which pathway you choose: you still owe the federal government the $202.90 per month Part B premium. Medicare Advantage does not replace this government fee — it only replaces the insurer.

---

## Original Medicare vs. Medicare Advantage: The Decision

<Diagram id="ch02_img16" />

Neither option is universally better. The right choice depends on your priorities.

Choose **Original Medicare + Medigap** if you travel frequently, want to see specialists at major academic medical centres across the country, or simply value the freedom of never worrying about networks. You will pay more each month in total premiums, but your out-of-pocket exposure on any given medical bill is near zero.

Choose **Medicare Advantage** if you want lower monthly costs and are comfortable with a local network of doctors. The built-in out-of-pocket cap protects you from catastrophic costs, drug coverage is usually included, and many plans offer extras like dental and vision. The risk is that if you need complex care at an out-of-network specialist, the plan may refuse to cover it.

Roughly half of all Medicare-eligible Americans now choose Medicare Advantage, making it the majority option — a shift that has fundamentally reshaped the health insurance industry over the past two decades.

## Which Path Are You On?

Put it all together and five clear paths emerge, determined almost entirely by your age, your employer, and your income.

<Diagram id="ch02_img17" />
