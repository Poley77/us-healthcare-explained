Everything we have covered in this book — the payers, the providers, the ACOs, the IPAs, the MSOs, the risk models, the quality scores, the RAF scores — is held together by contracts. Contracts are the infrastructure of the healthcare system. They define who is responsible for what, who gets paid how much and when, and what happens when things go wrong.

Most people in healthcare spend a lot of time inside one or two contracts. Few ever zoom out to see the full chain. This chapter does that.

---

## The Chain of Contracts

Value-based healthcare does not involve one contract. It involves a nested series of contracts, each one carrying obligations from the layer above down to the layer below.

<Diagram id="ch15_img01" />

At the top, CMS or another payer — an employer, a state <T>Medicaid</T> agency — establishes the terms of the program. For <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T>, this is a regulatory and contractual agreement between CMS and each <T>health plan</T>. For MSSP, it is a participation agreement between CMS and each ACO. For commercial insurance, it is an employer contract or a regulatory license. These are the foundational documents that set the rules everything else operates within.

The health plan then contracts with provider organizations. In a Medicare Advantage context, the plan may enter a value-based contract directly with an ACO or provider group, passing down a share of the capitated payment and some portion of the financial risk it received from CMS. In a <T>fee-for-service</T> context, it signs provider participation agreements establishing the rates it will pay for each service.

The ACO sub-contracts with the IPAs and physician groups that make up its network. These are often called participation agreements or <T>sub-<T>capitation</T></T> agreements. The ACO passes down a share of the PMPM it receives, along with performance obligations — quality targets, <T>care management</T> expectations, data-sharing requirements — that the physician network must meet for the ACO to earn its <T>shared savings</T>.

The IPA then contracts with its member physician practices. These agreements define how the capitation or shared savings will be distributed, what quality expectations apply to individual physicians, and how poor performance will be handled.

Finally, any practice working with an MSO will have a <T>Management Services Agreement</T> — an MSA — that defines what services the MSO provides, what it charges (usually a percentage of revenue or a flat fee), and what data and operational obligations both parties carry.

The patient sits entirely outside this chain. They have no contract. They receive care.

## What Each Party Is Looking For

Understanding contracts means understanding that every party at the table has a different set of priorities — and that a good contract creates alignment between them while a bad one creates friction that eventually undermines the whole arrangement.

<Diagram id="ch15_img03" />

CMS and health plans want the system to work efficiently: costs below <T>benchmark</T>, quality measures met, members retained, risk coded accurately, and reporting submitted on time. For an MA plan, Stars performance sits over everything — because a 4-star rating means a bonus payment worth potentially hundreds of millions of dollars. They want a network that will make them look good to CMS and to members. They want predictability.

What they are wary of: providers who take on risk they cannot manage, driving unanticipated costs; gaming of quality measures without genuine improvement; and <T>attribution</T> disputes that create data noise.

ACOs enter contracts wanting room to succeed. Specifically: a benchmark that is genuinely achievable given their population (not inflated by prior high utilization that they have already driven down); a savings share percentage that makes the investment in care management worthwhile; protection against catastrophic loss events through downside caps or <T>stop-loss</T> provisions; and clean, timely data from the payer so they can actually manage the population. Most ACOs starting out want upside-only arrangements — the chance to earn a bonus without risking a penalty — before they accept two-sided risk.

What they are wary of: attribution rules that allow patients they've been investing in to be reassigned to another ACO at year-end; benchmarks that reset downward every time they improve performance, shrinking their opportunity to earn savings (the so-called benchmark glide path problem); and quality measure sets that are so burdensome they consume more resources than the potential savings justify.

IPAs and provider groups want an adequate PMPM that covers the cost of caring for the patients they're being assigned, plus something left over for performance bonuses. They want attribution transparency — a clear picture of which patients they are responsible for, updated frequently enough to be actionable. They want stop-loss protection: a contractual ceiling on how much they can lose on any single catastrophically expensive patient (usually a high-cost reinsurance threshold, beyond which the plan absorbs the excess cost). And they want clean <T>claims</T> payment — not a bureaucratic maze of denials, audits, and clawbacks that erodes the economics they thought they had contracted for.

What they are wary of: vague or one-sided contract language that gives the plan discretion to reinterpret terms mid-year; inadequate data sharing that leaves them flying blind; and PMPM rates that look attractive but quietly include obligations — specialist carve-outs, pharmacy costs, facility costs — that make the contract unworkable in practice.

Physicians are often the last layer to see a contract's terms and the most directly affected by its real-world implications. At the individual practice level, the priorities are stability and fairness: a base revenue stream that is reliable, upside payment for quality performance that is genuinely attainable, an administrative burden that does not consume the margin they are supposed to earn, and confidence that they will not face unexpected clawbacks — demands to return previously paid funds because of a retroactive reconciliation. Clinical autonomy is also a real concern; physicians who feel that contracting arrangements are directing their clinical decisions in ways that conflict with their patients' interests will disengage, and an ACO full of disengaged physicians fails.

## The Timeline Problem

One of the most underappreciated features of VBC contracts is their timeline — and specifically, the long lag between doing the work and getting paid for it.

<Diagram id="ch15_img02" />

A typical MSSP or MA value-based contract runs on a calendar year: January 1 through December 31. Contracts are usually signed or renewed in the preceding autumn, often in October or November, with the performance period beginning January 1. Attribution — the assignment of patients to the ACO — is typically set at or near the start of the year based on prior utilization patterns, though it can shift during the year as patients' care patterns change.

Throughout the year, the ACO and its provider network are investing real money in care management, data infrastructure, quality improvement programs, and nurse navigators — none of which shows up as revenue until reconciliation. Payers report quarterly data on performance, which gives ACOs a view of their trajectory, but those reports are always months behind real-time.

When December 31 arrives, the performance year closes. But the money does not arrive. CMS takes months — sometimes six, sometimes twelve or more — to reconcile claims, finalize quality scores, calculate savings, and issue payments. For MSSP ACOs, reconciliation typically takes nine to fifteen months after year-end. That means an organization that performed well in 2024 may not receive its shared savings payment until mid-to-late 2025.

This lag has profound financial implications. ACOs and their provider networks must fund their care management operations out of current cash flow — fee-for-service revenue, sub-capitation PMPMs, or working capital — while waiting for savings that may or may not arrive, and that cannot be precisely predicted in advance. Smaller organizations without strong balance sheets struggle to sustain this. It is one of the reasons MSOs that can provide operational support and financial infrastructure are so valuable to physician-led ACOs — and one of the reasons private capital has flowed into the space. Investors can absorb the timeline mismatch in a way that a ten-physician practice cannot.

## Key Contract Terms Every Healthcare Professional Should Know

A fluent reader of VBC contracts will encounter several terms repeatedly. These are worth knowing:

Benchmark — the spending target against which an ACO's actual cost of care is measured. Set by CMS using historical data. The difference between actual and benchmark determines whether savings exist to share.

<T>Minimum Savings Rate</T> (MSR) — the threshold of savings an ACO must exceed before it can receive any shared savings. Designed to filter out random statistical variation. If an ACO's savings fall below the MSR, it receives nothing even if it technically beat the benchmark.

Savings sharing rate — the percentage of net savings the ACO keeps. Typically 50–75% depending on the track and risk level, with CMS retaining the remainder.

Stop-loss / reinsurance — a contractual provision, or separately purchased insurance, that caps the loss exposure on catastrophically expensive patients. Critical for any provider organization accepting <T>downside risk</T>.

<T>Clawback</T> — a demand by the payer to recover previously paid funds, typically triggered by a retroactive audit finding that claims were miscoded or overpaid. A major fear for providers in risk arrangements.

Network adequacy — a regulatory requirement that an MA plan or ACO maintains a sufficient number and type of providers to serve its enrolled population within reasonable geographic and time-access standards. Plans that fail network adequacy can face CMS sanctions.

Carve-out — services explicitly excluded from the capitation or risk arrangement and paid separately. Common carve-outs include pharmacy (PBM-administered), behavioral health, certain specialty services, and high-cost biologics. Understanding what is and is not carved out is essential to understanding the true economics of any capitated contract.

In lieu of services (ILOS) — a Medicaid managed care provision that allows MCOs to pay for non-covered services (like respite care, meal delivery, or home modifications) when doing so avoids a more expensive covered service. Increasingly used to address social determinants of health.

Value-based purchasing (VBP) arrangement — an umbrella term for any contract that links payment to quality or efficiency metrics, whether shared savings, <T>bundled payments</T>, capitation, or pay-for-performance bonuses.

## Why Contracts Are Where the System Either Works or Doesn't

The sophistication of a contract — and the trust between the parties to it — largely determines whether a <T>value-based care</T> arrangement delivers on its promise.

A well-designed contract creates aligned incentives: the plan wants quality and efficiency, the ACO wants achievable savings, the physicians want stable income and upside, and the patient receives better, better-coordinated care as a result. The interests reinforce each other.

A badly designed contract — with unachievable benchmarks, inadequate data, opaque attribution, buried carve-outs, or aggressive clawback provisions — produces the opposite. ACOs lose money, disengage from care management programs, or exit the arrangement entirely. Physicians feel exploited and revert to volume-maximizing behavior. The plan loses its network of quality providers. The patient sees none of the promised improvement.

This is why, as value-based care has matured, contract design has become a professional discipline in itself. Health plans employ teams of actuaries, network executives, and legal counsel to construct contracts that attract capable provider partners while protecting the plan's economics. ACOs and IPAs employ (or hire consultants with) contracting expertise to negotiate terms that make the arrangement viable. MSOs build contract management into their core service offering.

Contracts are not administrative paperwork. They are the rules of the game — and the game only produces good outcomes when the rules are set fairly.
