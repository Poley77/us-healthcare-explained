The previous three chapters introduced the ACO, the IPA, and the MSO as separate concepts. In practice, they rarely operate in isolation. They are three layers of the same system — each doing a distinct job, each dependent on the others.

This chapter brings them together.

---

## One Sentence Each

Before going further, here is the sharpest version of what each one does:

The ACO is the accountability structure. It holds the contract, bears the performance risk, and answers to the payer for quality and cost outcomes.

The IPA is the network structure. It organizes independent physicians into a single entity that can contract collectively, sub-contract within an ACO, and distribute payments to its members.

The MSO is the operational structure. It provides the infrastructure — data, billing, <T>care management</T>, technology, staffing — that makes it possible to actually perform under a value-based contract.

None of these is optional in a functioning VBC system. You need accountability, or no one is responsible for outcomes. You need a network, or there are no providers to deliver the care. You need infrastructure, or the accountability and the network have nothing to operate with.

## How They Stack in Practice

The most common real-world structure — particularly in <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> and MSSP — looks like this: a payer or CMS sits at the top, holding a contract with an ACO. The ACO sub-contracts with one or more IPAs, which organize the physician network. The MSO sits beneath all of them, providing the operational backbone that the ACO and IPA depend on to function.

Money flows down: from the payer to the ACO, from the ACO to the IPA, from the IPA to member practices. Accountability flows up: the practices are accountable to the IPA, the IPA to the ACO, the ACO to the payer. The MSO sits off to the side of this chain — not bearing risk, not holding contracts — but enabling every layer of it to function.

This is why the MSO is sometimes described as the operating system of <T>value-based care</T>. An operating system doesn't decide where the computer goes or what software runs on it. But without it, nothing works.

## The Lines Are Often Blurry

A critical thing to understand — and one that confuses many people new to the industry — is that these three functions do not always correspond to three separate organizations.

A single company can perform all three roles simultaneously. A physician group might form an ACO to hold a CMS contract, create an IPA to organize its network, and build or contract with an MSO to run its operations. An investor-backed platform might acquire an MSO, use it to support an IPA, and plug that IPA into an ACO contract — all within one corporate family.

This is why labeling organizations by type can be misleading. The better question is always: which of these functions is this organization performing, and for whom?

## Why the Confusion Persists

People working in healthcare often use these terms loosely, or use them to describe organizations that have evolved well beyond any single function. An "IPA" may have built its own data platform and effectively operates as an MSO for its members. An "MSO" may hold <T>capitation</T> contracts and function economically like an ACO. A <T>health plan</T> may own entities that perform all three functions vertically.

The framework — accountability, network, infrastructure — is not about labeling. It is about understanding what job is being done. When you encounter an organization you don't immediately recognize, ask: is this entity holding accountability, organizing a network, or running operations? Usually the answer points clearly to its role in the system — regardless of what it calls itself.

## The Patient at the Center

All of this complexity exists, ultimately, to serve a single purpose: getting better care to patients more efficiently.

The ACO creates the financial incentive to manage care well across a whole population. The IPA brings independent physicians into that accountability structure without forcing them to give up their practices. The MSO gives those physicians the infrastructure to actually perform — the data to see their patients clearly, the care managers to reach them proactively, the technology to track gaps and coordinate across providers.

A patient like Maria — from Chapter 1 — goes to her doctor's office and sees her physician. She doesn't know that her physician is a member of an IPA, which sub-contracts to an ACO, which has a risk contract with a Medicare Advantage plan, all supported by an MSO that flags her as high-risk and sends a care coordinator to call her next week. She just knows her doctor's office called to check in.

That call is the system working. The three layers behind it are how it was made possible.
