We have spent a great deal of this book explaining how <T>value-based care</T> works in concept — the contracts, the accountability structures, the organizations. Now it is time to follow the money itself. Where does it come from? Where does it go? How does it move through the system, and in what form does it arrive at the physician's practice?

The answer is more complex than most people realize — because in a mature VBC organization, money arrives from multiple directions, in multiple forms, on multiple timelines. Understanding all of them is essential to understanding the financial reality of modern healthcare.

---

## Two Fundamentally Different Ways Money Moves

Before getting into the specifics, it helps to understand the two basic models operating simultaneously in US healthcare.

In <T>fee-for-service</T>, money moves in response to activity. A patient visits a doctor. The doctor's office submits a claim — a bill, formatted in standardized codes, sent electronically to the payer. The payer's systems check coverage, confirm the service is payable at the contracted rate, and issue payment, typically within 14 to 30 days. No visit, no claim. No claim, no revenue. The provider is essentially running a transaction business: every transaction generates a discrete payment.

<Diagram id="ch16_img03" />

In value-based / capitated arrangements, money moves on a schedule, regardless of what happens clinically. The payer sends a fixed monthly payment per enrolled member — the PMPM — to the provider organization. It arrives whether the patient had three visits that month or none. It arrives whether the patient was healthy or was hospitalized twice. The provider receives the money upfront and must manage care within that budget over time.

This shift — from reactive payments triggered by services to proactive payments tied to enrollment — is the most fundamental economic change that value-based care introduces. It changes what providers track, what they invest in, how they think about risk, and how they manage cash.

It is worth noting that fee-for-service does not disappear in a capitated world. Most VBC organizations operate with both running simultaneously: a FFS layer for services not covered by the <T>capitation</T>, and a capitated layer for the primary care or <T>total cost of care</T> that is being managed under the VBC contract.

## The PMPM: How Capitation Arrives

The PMPM — <T>per member per month</T> — is the basic unit of payment in capitated healthcare. When a <T>health plan</T> or ACO pays a provider organization on a capitated basis, it calculates a monthly payment for each patient attributed to that provider and sends it each month.

PMPMs are not random numbers. They are calculated based on several factors: the local cost benchmarks set by CMS or the commercial payer, the health plan's contracted rate with the ACO, and — critically — the <T>RAF score</T> of the patients being attributed. A provider organization managing a sicker, more complex population will receive higher PMPMs, because those patients are expected to cost more to care for. This is the <T>risk adjustment</T> logic from Chapter 14, applied at the payment level.

For a primary care practice operating under a capitated MA contract, a realistic PMPM might range from $100 to $300 per patient per month for the primary care component alone, depending on the market, the population, and the plan. That payment must cover the physician's time, the practice's overhead, the <T>care management</T> infrastructure, the MSO's services, and a reserve against unexpected costs. How those dollars are allocated internally — and whether there is anything left over — determines the financial health of the practice.

## What the PMPM Has to Cover

Tracing where a capitated PMPM goes illuminates why the economics of VBC are so challenging to get right.

Starting from the top: CMS pays the MA plan a <T>benchmark</T> PMPM, risk-adjusted for each member. The plan keeps a portion — roughly 15% — for administration, profit, and regulatory reserves. A small additional amount may be reinvested from Stars bonus payments. The remainder flows to the provider network as <T>sub-capitation</T>.

Of that sub-capitation, the primary care layer typically receives 15–25% of the original CMS payment. From that amount, the physician practice must cover:

The physician's compensation — the actual cost of the clinical time and expertise being provided. In a capitated model, this is often expressed as a monthly amount per attributed patient rather than a per-visit fee.

The MSO management fee — if the practice is supported by an MSO, that organization charges for its services, typically either a flat monthly fee or a percentage of revenue (often 15–25%). This covers billing, analytics, care management staffing, technology, and operations.

Care management costs — nurse navigators, care coordinators, and the staff needed to proactively manage the panel. Some MA contracts pay a separate care management fee for this function, recognizing that the cost of proactive care management is distinct from the cost of delivering visits. These fees are typically $5–$30 PMPM, paid monthly on top of the base sub-capitation.

A risk reserve — a portion of the PMPM held back to cover unexpected utilization spikes, high-cost patients, or year-end reconciliation adjustments. Without a reserve, a bad quarter can create a cash crisis.

## <T>Shared Savings</T>: The Long-Tail Payment

On top of the PMPM, provider organizations in shared savings arrangements have the potential to earn an additional payment at year-end — the shared savings distribution.

Shared savings, as described in Chapter 7, are the result of actual total cost of care coming in below the benchmark that CMS or the health plan set at the start of the year. If an MSSP ACO was expected to spend $12,000 per attributed member and actually spent $11,200, it generated $800 per member in savings. CMS keeps a portion; the ACO receives the rest — typically 50–75%, subject to meeting minimum quality thresholds.

That savings payment then flows down through the ACO to participating IPAs and physician practices, according to the distribution formulas in their contracts. Practices that performed well on quality measures and managed utilization effectively typically receive a larger share. Those that generated excessive costs or poor quality scores may receive less.

The critical point is the timing: shared savings are not received until 9 to 15 months after the performance year closes. An ACO that performed well in 2024 may not see that payment until mid-to-late 2025. The practice has already paid its staff, its MSO, and its care managers. The savings payment, when it arrives, is essentially a retrospective bonus for a year of work already completed.

This timing mismatch is one of the central financial management challenges in VBC, and it is why organizations entering shared savings arrangements need either strong balance sheets, access to capital, or MSO partners who can bridge the gap.

## Care Management Fees

A category of payment that sits between the PMPM and shared savings — and is often overlooked — is the care management fee.

Several payer programs, particularly in <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> and <T>Medicaid</T> managed care, pay provider organizations a separate flat fee for providing enhanced care management services to specific patient populations. CMS itself has several mechanisms for this: <T>Chronic Care Management</T> (CCM) billing codes allow practices to bill monthly for care coordination services provided to patients with two or more chronic conditions. Transitional Care Management (TCM) codes provide payments for intensive follow-up after a hospital discharge. Principal Care Management (PCM) codes cover patients with a single high-complexity condition.

These are not PMPM payments — they are specific FFS billing codes that can coexist with capitated arrangements and add meaningful revenue to practices that invest in structured care management programs. A practice with 500 Medicare patients with qualifying chronic conditions, billing CCM at roughly $60–$120 per patient per month, can generate $30,000–$60,000 in monthly revenue from this source alone — on top of whatever capitated payments it receives.

More broadly, some MA plans and ACOs pay their provider networks a <T>population health</T> management fee — a separate monthly PMPM specifically to fund the operational infrastructure of care management. These fees, typically ranging from $5 to $30 PMPM, acknowledge that proactive population health management is expensive and should be separately compensated rather than buried within a primary care capitation rate.

## <T>Claims</T> Reimbursement: The Layer That Never Goes Away

Even in the most highly capitated VBC arrangements, claims — individual service-by-service billing — never disappear entirely. They remain the underlying mechanism for many payments, and they serve as the data record that drives risk adjustment, quality reporting, and cost measurement.

In a typical MA arrangement, a primary care practice may receive a capitated sub-PMPM for managing its attributed members' primary care. But those patients still see specialists, get hospitalized, use the emergency room, fill prescriptions, and receive imaging. All of those services generate FFS claims submitted to the MA plan, which pays them out of its retained budget. The primary care practice is generally not responsible for those costs under a basic sub-capitation arrangement — though in a more sophisticated shared risk contract, some portion of those downstream costs may flow back to affect the practice's shared savings calculation.

For services that are genuinely within the capitated scope, claims may still be submitted for tracking purposes even if they are not generating additional payment — these are called encounter data or shadow claims, and they are essential for populating the claims database that drives RAF scores, quality measurement, and ACO performance <T>attribution</T>.

## Revenue Streams in Practice: An Integrated View

What does this look like for a real primary care practice operating in the modern VBC ecosystem?

<Diagram id="ch16_img02" />

On any given month, a mature VBC-aligned primary care practice might receive: FFS claims payments from commercial and fee-for-service Medicare patients; a sub-capitated PMPM from one or more MA plans for attributed Medicare Advantage members; separate care management fees billed under CCM and TCM codes; a monthly population health management fee from their ACO; and — once a year, with a significant delay — a shared savings distribution if performance was strong.

Each of these streams has different timing, different volume drivers, different risk profiles, and different administrative requirements. Managing this financial complexity — understanding the mix, forecasting cash flow, reserving appropriately, investing in the care management infrastructure that drives the shared savings — is itself a sophisticated operational task. It is one of the most important things an MSO provides to the practices it supports.

## The Financial Logic of VBC

Stepping back, the financial architecture of value-based care creates a particular kind of economic story.

A provider organization in a capitated VBC arrangement is essentially running a healthcare management business. It receives a fixed monthly budget to keep a population of patients healthy. If it does its job well — managing chronic disease, preventing avoidable hospitalizations, coordinating care across specialists, closing quality gaps — it spends less than it receives and earns a margin. If it does its job poorly — or if the population turns out to be unexpectedly sick, or if the benchmark was set too low — it loses money.

This is a fundamentally different business model from fee-for-service, where the financial incentive is simply to see more patients and bill more claims. In VBC, the financial incentive is to keep patients healthy and out of expensive care settings. Whether that incentive reliably produces better care for patients is the central question of the value-based care movement — and it is one the healthcare system is still working to answer.
