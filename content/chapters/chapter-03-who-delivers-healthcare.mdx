In the last chapter, we followed the money — tracing how the government, employers, and health plans fund the care that Americans receive. Now we turn to the other side of that equation: the people and organizations actually delivering the care.

## The Front Door: Primary Care

For most patients, healthcare begins with a primary care physician — a doctor trained to be the first point of contact for almost any health concern. Primary care physicians handle routine checkups, manage chronic conditions like diabetes and high blood pressure, prescribe medications, and decide when a patient needs to see someone else.

That last function — deciding when to refer — makes primary care the connective tissue of the entire care delivery system. A good primary care doctor doesn't just treat what's in front of them. They coordinate, they watch for early warning signs, and they direct patients to the right specialists, labs, and hospitals when needed.

In <T>value-based care</T>, which we'll explore in Chapter 4, primary care takes on even greater importance. It is often the point where the most consequential and cost-effective interventions happen — catching a problem before it becomes a crisis, keeping a patient with heart disease out of the emergency room, helping someone manage a chronic condition so it doesn't escalate.

## Specialists, Labs, Hospitals

Beyond primary care, the rest of the care delivery system breaks into a few familiar categories.

Specialists are physicians with advanced training in a specific area — cardiology, oncology, orthopedics, neurology, and dozens more. Patients usually reach them through a referral from their primary care doctor, though in some insurance plans they can self-refer. Specialists handle the more complex clinical territory that falls outside primary care's scope.

Labs and imaging centers are the diagnostic infrastructure of the system. When a doctor needs to know what's actually happening inside a patient — blood work, an X-ray, an MRI, a biopsy — these are the facilities that make that possible.

Hospitals are where the most intensive — and most expensive — care happens. Emergency departments, surgeries, intensive care units, labor and delivery, complex inpatient care. A single hospital stay can generate more cost than months of outpatient care. This is why so much of the effort in value-based care is focused on keeping patients out of hospitals when a hospital stay isn't necessary.

## How Physicians Are Organized

Here is where it gets more nuanced — because while patients usually think of their doctor as an individual, physicians are organized in a variety of ways that have significant implications for how care gets delivered and how money flows.

Some physicians still practice independently — they own their own small practice, hire a small staff, and run their clinic as a business. Independent practice was once the norm. Today it is increasingly rare, squeezed by the administrative burden of billing, compliance, and technology costs that are simply easier to absorb at scale.

More commonly today, physicians work within medical groups — larger organizations that employ or affiliate multiple physicians, sometimes across multiple specialties and locations. Medical groups can negotiate better contracts with health plans, spread administrative costs across more physicians, and build the infrastructure needed to participate in value-based care programs.

At the largest scale are health systems — organizations that combine hospitals, employed physician groups, outpatient clinics, specialty practices, and sometimes insurance functions under one roof. The biggest health systems in the country — organizations like <T>Kaiser Permanente</T>, Cleveland Clinic, or Ascension — are enormous enterprises employing thousands of physicians and serving millions of patients.

The trend over the past two decades has been unmistakable: consolidation. Independent physicians joining medical groups. Medical groups merging or being acquired by health systems. Health systems growing through mergers. This consolidation has reshaped the economics of care delivery, giving larger organizations more negotiating power with insurers and more capacity to invest in the technology and staff needed to thrive in value-based care.

## Two Very Different Businesses

It is worth pausing to note that hospitals and physician practices, while both "delivering care," are actually very different businesses — with different cost structures, different payment models, and different incentives.

A physician practice's main costs are physicians, staff, and office overhead. Its revenue comes from relatively low-cost, high-volume outpatient encounters. A hospital's cost structure is far heavier — facilities, equipment, specialized staff available around the clock — and its revenue comes from the high-cost, lower-volume events that require that infrastructure.

This distinction matters because payers, particularly those running value-based care programs, often want to shift care from expensive hospital settings to lower-cost outpatient ones wherever it is clinically appropriate. A follow-up visit at a doctor's office, for instance, is far cheaper than the same visit conducted at a hospital outpatient clinic — even when the clinical content is identical.

## The Foundation of the System

The organizations in this chapter — physician practices, medical groups, hospitals, health systems — are where care actually happens. Everything else in the healthcare ecosystem exists to support, finance, organize, or improve what they do.

In the chapters ahead, we'll see how these organizations are being reshaped by new payment models, new accountability structures, and new layers of infrastructure designed to help them manage not just individual patients, but entire populations.
