Imagine you are a primary care physician running a small practice. You have two exam rooms, a nurse, and a front-desk coordinator. You see thirty patients a day, bill <T>fee-for-service</T>, and have been doing this for fifteen years.

Now a <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> <T>health plan</T> approaches you. They want to move to a value-based contract — <T>capitation</T>, <T>population health</T> targets, quality measures, data reporting requirements. They need a partner that can manage a panel of five thousand patients, track outcomes across the full care continuum, and take on financial risk.

You cannot do this alone. You do not have the data infrastructure, the <T>care management</T> staff, or the legal and financial capacity to take on that kind of contract. And even if you did, you only have a few hundred Medicare patients — nowhere near the scale that makes the economics work.

This is exactly the problem the <T>Independent Practice Association</T> was designed to solve.

---

## What an IPA Is

An IPA is a legal entity — typically a medical corporation or LLC — that independent physicians join in order to contract collectively with health plans, ACOs, and other payers.

The key word is independent. Physicians who join an IPA do not sell their practices. They do not become employees. They keep their own offices, their own staff, their own patient relationships, and their own day-to-day clinical autonomy. What they give up is a slice of their contracting independence — they agree to let the IPA negotiate on their behalf, and to abide by the contracts and performance standards the IPA accepts.

In exchange, they gain something they could not have on their own: scale.

## Origins: The Managed Care Era

IPAs did not originate in <T>value-based care</T>. They emerged in the 1970s and 1980s as a response to the rise of HMOs — Health Maintenance Organizations — which were demanding that physicians accept capitated payment structures and join organized networks.

Solo and small-group physicians faced a choice: join a staff-model HMO (and become employees) or figure out how to contract with HMOs while staying independent. The IPA model was the answer. By banding together, independent physicians could present a unified network to HMOs, negotiate rates collectively, and distribute capitation payments among themselves.

The managed care wave eventually subsided, and with it some of the urgency around IPAs. But the model never disappeared — and when value-based care began its ascent in the 2010s, IPAs found new relevance almost immediately. The same logic applied: health plans and ACOs wanted organized networks of physicians who could take on risk contracts. Independent physicians needed a vehicle to participate without being acquired.

## What an IPA Actually Does

At its core, an IPA performs three functions.

Network formation. The IPA recruits and credentiates physicians — primary care, specialists, and sometimes allied health professionals — into its network. Size and composition matter: a well-constructed IPA has enough primary care physicians to manage a meaningful patient population, the right mix of specialists to keep care in-network, and geographic coverage that matches the health plan's service area.

Contracting. The IPA enters into contracts with health plans, ACOs, or directly with CMS on behalf of its physician members. This is where the collective leverage pays off. A single primary care physician negotiating alone has almost no leverage with a major health plan. An IPA representing two hundred physicians across a region has real negotiating power — over rates, over quality measurement criteria, over the terms of risk arrangements.

Distribution and governance. When capitation payments or <T>shared savings</T> flow into the IPA, they must be distributed to member physicians according to the IPA's internal rules. This requires governance infrastructure — a board, a payment methodology, and a mechanism for tracking individual physician performance. High-performing physicians often receive a larger share. Those who generate excessive costs or poor quality scores may receive less, or face corrective action.

## The IPA and Value-Based Care

In a VBC context, IPAs often function as the network layer beneath an ACO. The ACO holds the contract with CMS or the health plan and is accountable for overall performance. The IPA organizes the physician network through which that care is actually delivered.

This is not a universal structure — some ACOs are physician-led and function similarly to IPAs themselves — but the pairing is common, particularly in markets where independent practice has remained strong, such as California, Texas, and Florida.

IPAs also play an important role in physician aggregation — the process by which private equity-backed and strategically-driven organizations build large physician networks by affiliating with many smaller IPAs. Rather than acquiring practices outright, an aggregator can contract with multiple IPAs and effectively assemble a large virtual network without the complexity of employment relationships. This model has become increasingly prominent in Medicare Advantage, where the economics of capitated contracts reward scale and network density.

## The Limits of the IPA Model

An IPA is fundamentally a contracting vehicle. It can negotiate a risk contract and distribute payments — but it cannot, by itself, provide the data analytics, care management programs, quality improvement infrastructure, and operational support that a physician network needs to actually perform under that contract.

This is the gap that the <T>Management Services Organization</T> — the MSO — was built to fill. And it is where we turn next.
