We have spent several chapters looking at the provider side of <T>value-based care</T> — ACOs, IPAs, MSOs, the organizations that deliver and coordinate care. Now we turn to the other side of the equation: the health plans that pay for it.

A <T>health plan</T> is, at its core, a <T>risk-bearing entity</T>. It collects money — from employers, individuals, or the government — and in exchange agrees to cover the cost of its members' healthcare. If actual costs come in below what it collected, it makes money. If costs run over, it absorbs the loss. Managing that gap is the central business of every health plan in America.

But not all health plans are the same. There are three distinct markets, each serving a different population, funded differently, and operating under different rules.

## Commercial Plans

Commercial health insurance covers the largest single group of Americans — roughly 160 million people who receive coverage through their employer or purchase it directly.

As described in Chapter 2, most large employers are technically self-insured: they bear the actual financial risk and hire an insurance company to administer their plan. Smaller employers typically buy fully-insured plans, transferring risk to the insurer in exchange for a premium.

Either way, the health plan's job on the commercial side is to build a network of providers, negotiate rates, process <T>claims</T>, and manage utilization — all while keeping the plan attractive enough that employers and employees choose it. Commercial plans primarily pay providers on a <T>fee-for-service</T> basis, though many are increasingly experimenting with value-based arrangements, particularly for high-cost conditions and primary care.

The dominant commercial insurers — UnitedHealthcare, Elevance Health (formerly Anthem), Aetna (CVS Health), Cigna — operate at enormous scale, covering tens of millions of members each and wielding significant negotiating leverage with providers as a result.

## <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T>

Medicare Advantage is the fastest-growing and, in many ways, the most consequential health plan market in the country. It now covers roughly half of all Medicare-eligible Americans — around 33 million people — and that share continues to climb.

The mechanics were introduced in Chapter 2: CMS pays private insurers a fixed monthly amount per enrollee, based on a risk-adjusted estimate of how much that person is expected to cost. The insurer takes on the responsibility of managing that person's care within that budget. If the insurer manages costs well, it profits. If not, it absorbs losses.

This structure creates very different incentives than commercial insurance. Because MA plans receive a fixed payment per member rather than collecting premiums against expected costs, they are naturally pushed toward managing care more actively — keeping members healthy, coordinating care, and avoiding unnecessary hospitalizations. This is why MA has become the proving ground for value-based care in the US, and why MA plans have been the most aggressive in entering risk-sharing arrangements with provider organizations.

The financial engine of MA — <T>risk adjustment</T>, Stars ratings, and bid mechanics — is important enough to warrant its own detailed treatment. That is the subject of the next chapter.

## <T>Medicaid</T> Managed Care

Most Americans think of Medicaid as a government-run program where the state pays doctors directly. That was largely true once. Today, roughly 70 percent of Medicaid beneficiaries are enrolled in Managed Care Organizations — private health plans that states have contracted with to administer Medicaid benefits.

The structure mirrors Medicare Advantage in its basic logic: the state (using a mix of state and federal funds) pays the MCO a capitated rate <T>per member per month</T>, and the MCO manages care within that budget. States set the rules — eligibility, covered benefits, quality requirements — and CMS sets the federal floor. Beyond that, each state designs its own program, which is why Medicaid managed care looks quite different from state to state.

For the large commercial insurers — UnitedHealthcare, Centene, Molina, Elevance — Medicaid managed care is a major business line, not a footnote. Centene, in particular, built its entire business around Medicaid and has become one of the largest health plans in the country as a result.

Medicaid MCOs pay providers primarily on a fee-for-service basis, though states are increasingly building VBC requirements and quality incentives into their MCO contracts. The populations served — low-income adults, children, people with disabilities, individuals with complex behavioral health needs — present distinct <T>care management</T> challenges, and states are under growing pressure from CMS to demonstrate that managed care is delivering better outcomes, not just administrative efficiency.

## What Health Plans Have in Common

Across all three markets, health plans perform the same core functions: they collect money, assume risk, build provider networks, process claims, manage utilization, and report on quality. The differences are in who funds them, which population they serve, and what regulatory regime they operate under.

One trend cuts across all three markets: health plans are increasingly moving beyond pure insurance into care delivery. <T>UnitedHealth Group</T> owns <T>Optum</T>, which employs tens of thousands of physicians. CVS Health owns MinuteClinics and HealthHUBs as well as Aetna. Humana acquired a large home health company. The line between insurer and provider — once clear — is blurring deliberately, as the largest plans seek to capture more of the value chain and more directly control the care their members receive.

This <T>vertical integration</T> trend, and its implications for providers and patients alike, is one of the defining structural forces in American healthcare today.
