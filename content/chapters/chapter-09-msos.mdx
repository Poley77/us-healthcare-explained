An ACO can hold a risk contract. An IPA can organize a physician network. But neither one, by itself, can answer the question that determines whether a <T>value-based care</T> arrangement actually succeeds:

Who does the work?

Who builds the dashboards that show which patients are overdue for a diabetes screening? Who hires and manages the care coordinators who call high-risk patients before they end up in the emergency room? Who handles the billing, the quality measure reporting, the technology integrations, the payer negotiations, the HR compliance? Who distributes the <T>shared savings</T> when they arrive — and calculates who gets what?

This is what the <T>Management Services Organization</T> does. It is the operational engine that makes value-based care function in practice.

---

## What an MSO Is

An MSO is a company that provides administrative, operational, and technology services to physician practices, IPAs, and ACOs — typically under a long-term <T>management services agreement</T>.

The MSO does not deliver clinical care. It does not employ physicians (in most structures). What it does is provide everything around the clinical care that a physician practice needs to survive and perform in a value-based environment but would struggle to build on its own.

The scope of MSO services varies widely. Some MSOs are narrowly focused — revenue cycle management, or technology implementation, or quality reporting. Others are comprehensive platforms that effectively run the entire back office of a physician network, from HR to data analytics to <T>care management</T> to contracting. These full-service MSOs are sometimes described as providing "practice-in-a-box" infrastructure: everything a physician needs to operate except the clinical judgment itself.

## The <T>Corporate Practice of Medicine</T> — Why MSOs Exist

To understand why the MSO structure exists as a separate legal entity from the physician practice, you need to understand a foundational piece of US healthcare law: the Corporate Practice of Medicine doctrine, known as CPOM.

In most US states, corporations are prohibited from owning medical practices or directly employing physicians. The rationale is that clinical decisions should be made by licensed physicians exercising independent medical judgment — not by business executives optimizing for profit. Only a licensed physician can own a medical practice.

This creates a structural problem for anyone who wants to invest capital in building physician networks or bring sophisticated business management to primary care. You cannot simply buy a doctor's office the way you might acquire any other business.

The MSO structure solves this. The physician-owned <T>Professional Corporation</T> (PC) holds the medical license and employs the physicians. The MSO — which can be investor-owned, backed by private equity, or operated by a health system — provides all the non-clinical services under a management services agreement, typically in exchange for a management fee. The two entities are legally separate, but economically intertwined.

<Diagram id="ch09_img02" />

This is the structural backbone of much of the private investment that has flowed into value-based care. When a private equity firm "invests in a physician group," what they almost always mean is that they have acquired or built an MSO that provides services to a physician-owned PC. The physicians retain nominal ownership of the clinical entity. The investor owns the business infrastructure around it.

## The Four Things That Matter Most

While MSOs provide a wide range of services, four capabilities are most critical to VBC performance.

Data and analytics is the foundation. An MSO running a <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> or MSSP ACO must know, at any given moment, which patients are high-risk, which have gaps in care, which are likely to be hospitalized, and how the network is performing against its quality and cost benchmarks. This requires aggregating data from multiple sources — <T>claims</T> feeds from the <T>health plan</T>, <T>EHR</T> data from practices, lab and pharmacy data — cleaning it, and making it actionable. Without this, <T>population health</T> management is guesswork.

Care management is how the data gets acted on. Knowing that a patient with congestive heart failure hasn't had a follow-up appointment in three months is only useful if someone calls them. MSOs employ care coordinators, nurse navigators, social workers, and other non-physician staff whose job is to proactively reach patients, close care gaps, manage transitions out of the hospital, and support patients with complex needs. This infrastructure is expensive to build and rarely makes sense for a small practice to run independently.

Billing and revenue cycle includes not just claims submission but also <T>risk adjustment</T> coding — ensuring that the diagnoses documented in patient charts are accurately reflected in the codes submitted to payers. In Medicare Advantage and other capitated models, the monthly payment a provider receives is partly determined by the diagnosed risk of their patients. Accurate coding is therefore a meaningful revenue lever, and MSOs that specialize in this function can significantly improve the financial performance of the networks they support.

Technology underpins all of it — EHR integrations, data aggregation platforms, <T>care gap</T> dashboards, quality reporting tools, and the communication systems that connect care teams to patients. Health systems have IT departments for this. Small physician practices do not. The MSO fills that gap.

## MSOs and the Flow of Capital

The MSO has become one of the primary vehicles through which private capital has entered value-based primary care. Companies like Agilon Health, Privia Health, and Evolent Health have built large-scale MSO platforms serving hundreds of physician practices and millions of patients. Private equity firms have invested heavily in building or acquiring MSOs, drawn by the combination of recurring management fee revenue and the economic upside of the VBC contracts those MSOs support.

This capital flow has materially accelerated the adoption of value-based care infrastructure — bringing data platforms, care management programs, and operational expertise to physician networks that couldn't have built these capabilities alone. It has also raised legitimate questions about how financial incentives align when investor-owned companies are managing the operational infrastructure of clinical care.

Those tensions are real, but they are secondary to the fundamental point: in modern US healthcare, the MSO is not optional infrastructure. It is the operating layer that makes value-based care possible at scale.
