The previous chapter examined organizations that help physicians participate in <T>value-based care</T> while keeping most of the financial risk at arm's length. Aledade enables ACOs but earns its money from <T>shared savings</T>, not from carrying insurance risk. Privia creates a physician group with VBC capabilities, but its primary revenue model is a management fee.

This chapter examines two organizations that go further — stepping directly into the financial risk that VBC arrangements create, and building business models in which absorbing that risk is the core product.

---

## The Concept of a <T>Risk-Bearing Entity</T>

A risk-bearing entity is an organization that accepts financial responsibility for the <T>total cost of care</T> for a defined population, in exchange for a fixed payment. It is, in economic terms, behaving like an insurer — taking in a premium (the <T>capitation</T>) and managing costs to stay below it.

This is different from a platform that supports physicians in taking risk. A risk-bearing entity is the entity taking risk. It sits between the <T>health plan</T> and the physician network, accepting the capitated payment from the plan and then managing how that money is deployed across the care system.

The appeal for physician groups is significant: they get the upside of VBC — the potential to earn above their FFS revenue when care is well-managed — without needing to directly absorb the financial complexity of holding insurance risk, hiring actuaries, or managing <T>claims</T> reserves. The risk-bearing entity handles all of that. The appeal for investors is also significant: if the model works, the economics of taking in large capitated payments and managing costs below them can be very attractive.

The two companies in this chapter represent the most prominent examples of risk-bearing platforms built for <T><T>Medicare</T> Advantage</T> — and they took meaningfully different approaches.

---

## agilon health: The Joint Venture Model

agilon health was founded in 2016 with a distinctive thesis: the best way to build a Medicare Advantage VBC business is to partner deeply with established local physician groups, rather than building or acquiring practices from scratch. Local physicians know their communities. They have decades of patient relationships. They understand the social and clinical context of their population in ways that a nationally scaled company never could. What they lack is the capital, the technology, and the risk management expertise to take on <T>global capitation</T>.

agilon's model is built around a joint venture structure.

<Diagram id="ch19_img01" />

When agilon enters a new market, it identifies an established physician group with a strong Medicare patient panel and proposes a partnership. The physician group joins the joint venture and continues practicing as it always has. agilon contributes the MSO infrastructure — the analytics platform, the <T>care management</T> programs, the <T>population health</T> support, the financial risk management — and more importantly, it contributes capital and takes on the full financial risk of the capitated contract.

The economics work like this: an MA plan in the market pays agilon a global capitation PMPM — a fixed monthly payment per attributed Medicare member, covering the total expected cost of that member's care. agilon then becomes responsible for all of that care — primary, specialist, hospital, pharmacy. If the total cost of care comes in below the capitation received, agilon has generated a surplus. That surplus is shared with the physician group through the joint venture agreement. If costs come in above the capitation, agilon absorbs the loss.

This is a fundamentally different risk posture than Aledade or Privia. agilon is not earning a management fee or a share of savings on top of a FFS baseline. It is taking in a fixed revenue pool and managing against total cost of care. Its financial performance is directly linked to how efficiently its physician partners manage their patients.

What agilon provides to its physician partners is comprehensive: analytics and population health tools, care management staff, quality improvement programs, payer contracting support, actuarial modeling, and financial reporting. Physicians continue practicing independently under their own brand and maintain ownership of their practices. The joint venture is agilon's mechanism for aligning financial interests — the physician group participates in the surplus when the model performs well.

agilon went public in 2021 and grew rapidly, operating in dozens of markets across the US with physician partners covering hundreds of thousands of Medicare Advantage members. It has experienced, as most risk-bearing entities eventually do, the challenges of this model: medical cost inflation, an unexpectedly sick attributed population in some markets, and the actuarial difficulty of accurately pricing risk across many geographies. These challenges led to significant financial pressure and a period of restructuring — illustrating that taking on full global capitation risk is genuinely hard, and that the economics only work when the combination of capitation adequacy, population health management, and market execution all align.

## Alignment Health: The <T>Payvider</T> Model

Alignment Health takes the integration one step further. Where agilon sits between a health plan and a physician group — accepting risk from the plan, distributing management to the physicians — Alignment has built its own licensed Medicare Advantage health plan. It both pays for care and delivers it. In the industry, organizations that operate on both sides of the insurer-provider divide are called payviders.

Founded in 2013 and operating primarily in California, Nevada, North Carolina, and a handful of other markets, Alignment Health's core structure is straightforward but represents a significant operational undertaking: at the top is Alignment Health Plan, a licensed insurer that contracts with CMS to offer MA coverage and collects the risk-adjusted PMPM. Below that sits Alignment Medical Group — an employed physician organization — along with a network of contracted specialists, a telehealth service, and home-based care programs.

<Diagram id="ch19_img02" />

Because Alignment both collects the insurance premium and employs the physicians delivering care, the financial incentives on both sides of the equation are held by the same organization. An MA plan that contracts with an external physician group must negotiate, manage information asymmetries, and deal with the reality that the physician group's financial interests may not always align with the plan's. Alignment eliminates that friction by design: the plan's interest in managing cost efficiently and the care team's interest in providing good care are held within the same organizational structure.

This integration creates significant data advantages. Because Alignment controls both the insurance and the clinical side, it has full real-time visibility into utilization — not just claims data arriving with a 60-day lag, but clinical data from its own employed physician practices updating continuously. This allows Alignment's care management operations to intervene faster and more precisely than a health plan dependent on claims feeds.

Alignment has also invested heavily in home-based care and telehealth as a clinical strategy. For a Medicare Advantage population — predominantly seniors with multiple chronic conditions — the ability to deliver care at home reduces the friction of receiving care, improves adherence, and can prevent the kind of deterioration that leads to emergency department visits. Home visits and 24/7 telehealth access are core components of Alignment's member experience, and they contribute to both quality performance and cost management.

The payvider model carries its own risks, of course. Operating a licensed insurance company is capital-intensive and heavily regulated. MA plan economics are sensitive to Star ratings, risk coding accuracy, and annual CMS rate adjustments — any of which can materially shift revenue. And building an employed physician workforce from scratch in multiple markets requires sustained capital investment before the model reaches the scale needed to operate efficiently.

## Why These Models Represent a Strategic Bet

Both agilon and Alignment are making the same fundamental strategic bet — that the economics of Medicare Advantage global capitation are attractive enough to justify taking on full insurance risk, and that organizations with better data, better care management, and better clinical operations will generate enough surplus to build durable, profitable businesses.

That bet has proven highly contingent on execution. The years 2022–2024 were difficult for many risk-bearing entities in MA, as medical cost trends accelerated beyond what actuarial models had anticipated. Utilization patterns shifted after COVID-19 in ways that were difficult to predict. CMS made changes to <T>risk adjustment</T> methodology that compressed capitation rates. Organizations that had built business plans around certain medical loss ratios found themselves under significant pressure.

This does not mean the model is broken. It means that taking on insurance risk is hard — as every insurer has always known. The organizations that succeed over time in this space are those that combine strong clinical operations, disciplined actuarial pricing, precise risk coding, and the organizational culture to continuously adapt to changing conditions.

## The Spectrum: Four Models in Context

Looking across the four companies in Chapters 18 and 19, a clear spectrum emerges.

<Diagram id="ch19_img03" />

Aledade sits at the low-risk end: it is a platform provider, earning from shared savings, with the physician practice bearing its own FFS revenue as a floor. Privia moves slightly up the spectrum, with a group practice structure and modest VBC downside. agilon goes further still — it is the risk-bearing entity itself, absorbing full global capitation risk. Alignment goes furthest, operating as both insurer and care provider with no external risk transfer.

Each position on this spectrum reflects a different view of where the sustainable competitive advantage lies in VBC, and a different tolerance for financial exposure. None of these is the definitive right answer. What the spectrum illustrates is that value-based care is not a single business model — it is a family of related models organized around a common insight: that paying for outcomes rather than volume, and managing the health of populations rather than the volume of transactions, is a better way to organize healthcare.

Whether that insight translates reliably into better outcomes for patients is, in many ways, the most important question the entire industry is trying to answer.
