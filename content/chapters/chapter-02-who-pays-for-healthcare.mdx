Before you can understand anything else about the US healthcare system, you need to grasp one foundational fact.

In the United States, almost nobody pays directly for their own healthcare.

When you go to a doctor, someone is clearly paying — a bill gets generated, money changes hands. But the person in the exam room is almost never writing the check. The cost of that visit is routed through a third party — a government program, an employer, a <T>health plan</T> — before it ever reaches the doctor or hospital that provided the care.

This is the defining structural feature of American healthcare. Everything else flows from it.

There are essentially four sources of healthcare funding: the federal government, state governments, employers, and individuals paying out of pocket. Together, they cover almost all the care delivered in the country. The two biggest, by a wide margin, are the government and employers.

## <T>Medicare</T>: The Government Covers Older Americans

In 1965, President Lyndon Johnson signed Medicare into law. The problem it solved was simple and brutal: private insurers were largely refusing to cover people over 65, because older people are more expensive to insure. Millions of elderly Americans had no coverage at all.

Medicare created a federal guarantee: if you are 65 or older, or have a qualifying disability, you are covered. Today it covers roughly 66 million Americans and is administered by the <T>Centers for Medicare & <T>Medicaid</T> Services</T> — CMS — a federal agency that is the single most powerful entity in US healthcare. When CMS changes how it pays for something, the entire industry adjusts.

Medicare is divided into parts you will hear about constantly. Part A covers hospital care. Part B covers outpatient visits and doctor care. Part D covers prescription drugs. And Part C — <T>Medicare Advantage</T> — is something we will come back to shortly, because it has quietly transformed the industry.

## Medicaid: The Government Covers Lower-Income Americans

Medicaid was created alongside Medicare in 1965, but it works very differently. Where Medicare is a purely federal program, Medicaid is a federal-state partnership — the federal government provides funding and sets broad rules, but each state runs its own program. Eligibility, covered services, and administration vary significantly from state to state.

Medicaid covers roughly 90 million Americans — more than Medicare — and its population is different: low-income adults and children, pregnant women, people with disabilities, and elderly individuals who need long-term care. It is also, quietly, the largest single funder of nursing home and long-term care in the country, which surprises most people when they first learn it.

## Commercial Insurance: Employers Enter the Picture

If the government covers older and lower-income Americans, who covers everyone else?

Largely, employers do — and this too came about through historical accident. During World War II, the government imposed wage controls to prevent inflation. Employers couldn't compete for workers by paying more, but health benefits weren't classified as wages and were exempt. Offering health coverage became a way to attract talent, the practice stuck, and eventually became entrenched.

Today, employer-sponsored insurance covers roughly 160 million Americans. But your employer isn't actually providing your healthcare — they're buying insurance on your behalf from a health plan, a company whose job is to manage the financial risk of covering your medical costs.

The big health insurers — UnitedHealthcare, Aetna (now part of CVS Health), and Humana — built their businesses doing exactly this. An employer comes to them with 5,000 employees; the insurer designs a plan, sets premiums, builds a provider network, processes <T>claims</T>, and manages costs. In exchange, they collect premiums and, if they manage well, keep the margin.

## Self-Insured Employers: A Twist Worth Knowing

Large employers eventually noticed something. If their workforce turned out to be healthier than average, the insurer kept the profit. The employer got nothing back.

So large employers started self-insuring — setting aside their own money to pay employees' medical claims directly, rather than buying traditional insurance. They take on the financial risk themselves. If their workforce stays healthy, they save money.

But they still need someone to administer the plan — process claims, build a provider network, handle customer service. So they hire an insurer to do that administrative work without transferring the financial risk. This is called an <T>Administrative Services Only</T> (ASO) arrangement.

The result: even when you see a UnitedHealthcare or Aetna card in someone's wallet, the employer behind that card may be carrying the actual financial risk, not the insurer. Today, the majority of Americans with large-employer coverage are in self-insured plans — a fact that surprises almost everyone who hears it for the first time.

## Medicare Advantage: Where Government and Private Insurance Converge

This is one of the most important developments in healthcare over the past two decades, and central to understanding how the major health plans became the giants they are today.

<T>Traditional Medicare</T> is run directly by the government. CMS pays the bills and carries all the financial risk.

Medicare Advantage (Part C) introduced an alternative. Private insurers could offer Medicare-eligible patients a plan that covered all standard Medicare benefits — often with extras like dental and vision — bundled into something that felt simpler. In exchange, CMS would pay those insurers a fixed monthly amount per enrollee and let the insurer manage that person's care.

The insurer takes on the financial risk. If they manage care well and costs come in below the payment, they profit. If not, they absorb the loss.

This model has grown explosively. Today, roughly half of all Medicare-eligible Americans are enrolled in a Medicare Advantage plan rather than traditional Medicare. The companies that built the biggest Medicare Advantage businesses — UnitedHealthcare, Humana, and CVS Health through its Aetna subsidiary — became some of the most powerful organizations in American healthcare.

## The ACA: Filling the Gaps

Even with Medicare, Medicaid, and employer coverage, tens of millions of Americans fell through the cracks — people who didn't qualify for government programs but whose employers didn't offer coverage, or who were self-employed or worked part-time.

The <T>Affordable Care Act</T> of 2010 created online marketplaces where these individuals could shop for and purchase coverage, with subsidies to make it affordable. It also expanded Medicaid eligibility in participating states.

The ACA didn't replace any existing coverage structure. It added another layer — another ring of coverage around the populations the older layers had missed.

## The Players You'll Keep Hearing About

Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) runs Medicare and sets federal rules for Medicaid. It is the closest thing to a central authority in American healthcare, and every major health plan watches it closely. Changes to CMS payment rates and rules ripple through the entire industry.

UnitedHealthcare is the largest US health insurer by enrollment and revenue. Its parent company, <T>UnitedHealth Group</T>, also owns <T>Optum</T> — a massive health services and analytics business — which signals something important: the biggest health plans are no longer just paying for care. They are increasingly involved in delivering it.

Humana built itself into one of the largest Medicare Advantage insurers in the country. It is, in many ways, a Medicare specialist — its business is deeply tied to the senior population.

CVS Health is one of the most interesting transformations in healthcare. A pharmacy chain that became a health insurer when it acquired Aetna for $70 billion in 2018, CVS now operates pharmacies, pharmacy benefit management, insurance, and a growing network of primary care clinics — a business intentionally built to touch patients at multiple points in their care journey.

## Putting It Together

The federal government covers older and disabled Americans through Medicare. Federal and state governments together cover lower-income Americans through Medicaid. Employers and employees fund coverage for working-age adults through commercial insurance. And individuals outside those groups can buy coverage through ACA marketplaces.

Across every one of these channels, private health plans play a central role — building networks, processing claims, managing risk, and increasingly shaping how care is actually delivered.

This is the foundation. Every contract, every organization, every payment model in the chapters ahead is built on top of it.
