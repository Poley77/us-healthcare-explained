The previous chapters built a framework for understanding how <T>value-based care</T> works — the contracts, the money, the data, the organizations. This chapter puts that framework to work on two real companies that have built large, influential businesses by helping independent physicians succeed in value-based care.

Aledade and Privia Health both exist to solve the same fundamental problem: independent primary care physicians are increasingly being asked to take on <T>population health</T> accountability, but most of them lack the infrastructure, capital, and expertise to do it alone. Both companies stepped in to fill that gap. They did it differently — and understanding those differences illuminates the range of models that the VBC ecosystem has produced.

---

## Aledade: The ACO Enablement Model

Aledade was founded in 2014 by Farzad Mostashari, a former National Coordinator for Health Information Technology at HHS. The founding insight was simple: the infrastructure needed to succeed in <T>Medicare</T> ACOs — data analytics, <T>care management</T>, quality reporting, payer contracting expertise, regulatory navigation — was completely out of reach for the small independent practices that CMS was hoping would join the MSSP. Aledade would build that infrastructure and share it across a large network of independent physicians.

The model is deliberately designed to preserve physician independence. When a physician joins an Aledade ACO, they do not sell their practice. They do not become employees. They sign a participation agreement that gives Aledade the authority to contract on their behalf and access to their clinical data, and in exchange they receive the full platform of support services that Aledade provides.

<Diagram id="ch18_img01" />

Aledade's ACO functions are central: it applies to CMS to operate <T>Medicare <T>Shared Savings</T> Program</T> ACOs, manages the <T>attribution</T> of patients, monitors performance against the <T>benchmark</T>, and handles all regulatory and reporting obligations. Its MSO functions sit behind this: data integration with practice EHRs, population health analytics, <T>care gap</T> identification, care management support (including nurse navigators who work with member practices), and quality reporting preparation. Its technology platform — a custom-built analytics application — is the interface through which physicians see their attributed patients, their quality scores, and their projected shared savings trajectory.

How Aledade makes money is worth understanding because it aligns incentives cleanly. Aledade earns a share of the shared savings its ACOs generate — typically a percentage negotiated with each participating practice. If the ACO doesn't save money, Aledade doesn't earn a performance payment. The company does charge a modest practice fee to cover operating costs, but the bulk of its economics are tied to the savings it produces. This creates a direct incentive for Aledade to invest in the care management and analytics capabilities that actually move the needle on cost and quality.

By 2024, Aledade had grown to become one of the largest ACO operators in the country, supporting hundreds of ACOs, thousands of practices, and millions of Medicare patients across nearly every state. Its model has been widely studied and largely validated: its ACOs have consistently generated meaningful shared savings in the MSSP.

## What Aledade Looks Like in Practice

To make this concrete: imagine a three-physician primary care practice in rural Tennessee. The physicians have been seeing Medicare patients for years, billing <T>fee-for-service</T>, managing a full schedule. They have heard about value-based care and know vaguely that ACOs exist, but they have no data analytics capability, no care management staff, no expertise in CMS regulations, and no capacity to negotiate contracts with health plans.

Aledade approaches them, walks them through the model, and offers to enroll them in an MSSP ACO. The practice signs a participation agreement. Aledade integrates with their <T>EHR</T> (let's say they use eClinicalWorks), begins pulling <T>claims</T> data from the relevant payers, and gives the physicians access to a dashboard showing their attributed Medicare patients — including which patients have open care gaps, which have recently been hospitalized, and which are highest-risk.

The practice's day-to-day clinical work changes modestly: physicians are asked to use specific quality measure documentation in their notes, to follow up on care gap alerts, and to participate in periodic performance reviews with the Aledade care management team. A nurse navigator may be assigned to call the practice's highest-risk patients proactively.

At year-end, if the ACO has performed well — costs below benchmark, quality thresholds met — CMS pays out the shared savings. After the 9–15 month reconciliation lag, a distribution arrives. The practice receives its share, minus Aledade's fee. The three physicians, who invested nothing beyond their time and data access, have earned a meaningful bonus for work they largely would have done anyway — but now better organized and better measured.

## Privia Health: The Physician Group Model

Privia Health takes a structurally different approach. Founded in 2007 and now publicly traded, Privia operates as a multi-state physician group — not just a platform that supports independent practices, but an organization that physicians actually join, becoming employed members of the Privia Medical Group.

The distinction matters significantly because of CPOM — the <T>Corporate Practice of Medicine</T> doctrine introduced in Chapter 9. Privia the company is a publicly traded corporation. In most US states, corporations cannot employ physicians or own medical practices. Privia navigates this through state-specific Professional Corporations (PCs): in Virginia and DC, there is a Privia Medical Group of Virginia PC; in Texas, a separate entity; in California, another. These PCs are technically physician-owned legal entities. Privia the MSO provides all the services, but the physicians are employed by the PC — not by Privia directly.

<Diagram id="ch18_img02" />

Within this structure, Privia provides a comprehensive MSO platform: centralized billing and revenue cycle management, population health analytics, care management programs, quality reporting infrastructure, and payer contracting on behalf of its physician network. Physicians who join Privia effectively outsource their entire administrative operation to the platform, keeping clinical autonomy while accessing the scale advantages of a large multispecialty network.

How Privia makes money differs from Aledade's model. Privia charges a management services fee — typically a percentage of the physician practice's revenue, often in the range of 20–25%. This is a more predictable, volume-based revenue stream than shared savings, and it gives Privia economics that are less dependent on the annual ACO performance cycle. Privia also participates in shared savings and VBC contracts, but the MSO fee is the primary revenue driver.

Privia's network spans primary care and a range of specialties across multiple markets, and it contracts with both government payers (Medicare, <T>Medicaid</T>) and commercial plans. This multi-payer capability distinguishes it from organizations focused solely on Medicare.

## The Same Problem, Two Different Philosophies

Both Aledade and Privia exist to give independent physicians access to VBC infrastructure they cannot build alone. But they represent genuinely different philosophies about how to do that.

<Diagram id="ch18_img03" />

Aledade's approach is non-acquisitive: physicians keep everything, share data and contracting authority, and participate in the upside of savings without giving up ownership or independence. The relationship is fundamentally a services agreement. This approach scales well and preserves the physician's autonomy, but it also means Aledade has limited control over how physicians actually practice — the platform can show a care gap, but it cannot force a physician to close it.

Privia's approach is integrative: physicians join the organization, gain the benefits of scale and infrastructure, and are part of a larger economic and clinical community. This creates more alignment and potentially more operational leverage — but it also requires physicians to give up some independence and accept a more structured practice environment.

Neither model is inherently better. They serve different physician preferences and different market contexts. What they share is the conviction that independent primary care physicians — who remain the backbone of the US healthcare system despite decades of consolidation pressure — can succeed in value-based care if they have the right infrastructure and support.

## What These Models Illustrate About the Ecosystem

Looking at Aledade and Privia through the lens of the book's framework, several things become clear.

Both companies simultaneously perform ACO functions (holding or supporting VBC contracts, managing quality accountability, distributing shared savings), IPA functions (aggregating physicians into a contracting network, negotiating with payers on their behalf), and MSO functions (providing the operational and technology infrastructure that makes performance possible). The three roles are not separate organizations — they are three layers of one integrated platform.

Both companies derive their value from data. The analytics platform is not a nice-to-have feature; it is the product. Without the ability to see the population, identify risk, close care gaps, and track performance against benchmark, neither company can justify its existence to physicians or justify its economics to investors.

And both companies depend on the contract chain described in Chapter 15 being well-constructed. Their business models only work if the ACO contracts are profitable, if the benchmark is achievable, if the shared savings are distributed fairly, and if the payer relationships are sustainable over multiple years.

The physician-led model — at its best — represents something genuinely promising in American healthcare: independent physicians, supported by sophisticated infrastructure, taking responsibility for their patients' health across a whole year rather than one visit at a time. Whether that promise is consistently realized, and at what scale, is one of the central questions the healthcare industry is still working to answer.
