By the late 2000s, the problem with <T>fee-for-service</T> medicine was well understood. Nobody was accountable for the whole patient. The primary care doctor was paid for the visit. The specialist was paid for the procedure. The hospital was paid for the admission. If a patient fell through the gaps — missed a follow-up, skipped a medication, ended up in the ER for something preventable — no one in the system bore any financial consequence.

The <T>Accountable Care Organization</T> was designed to fix that.

## What an ACO Is

An <T>ACO</T> is a network of doctors, hospitals, and other providers that collectively agrees to be held accountable for the quality and <T>total cost of care</T> for a defined population of patients.

That word *accountable* is doing the work. The ACO doesn't just deliver care — it takes responsibility for how well that care is coordinated, how efficiently it is delivered, and whether the patients in its network stay healthier over time. If it succeeds, it shares in the savings. If it fails, it may owe money back.

<Diagram id="ch07_img01" />

ACOs can take many forms — hospital-led, physician-led, hybrid — but the underlying logic is consistent: bring providers together under a shared accountability structure, give them data and incentives to manage care more effectively, and link their financial results to how well they do it.

---

## A Brief History

The ACO concept was formalized by the <T>Affordable Care Act</T> in 2010, which created the <T>Medicare Shared Savings Program</T> — <T>MSSP</T> — as the primary vehicle for ACOs in traditional Medicare. By bringing a network of providers into a contractual relationship with <T>CMS</T>, MSSP ACOs could earn a share of savings if they reduced costs below their <T>benchmark</T> while meeting quality thresholds.

In 2022, CMS launched <T>ACO REACH</T> — Realizing Equity, Access, and Community Health — as a more advanced, higher-risk successor to earlier ACO models. ACO REACH introduced <T>global capitation</T> options, stronger <T>health equity</T> requirements, and a structure designed to attract more sophisticated risk-bearing organizations.

## The Four Mechanics That Drive ACO Performance

Understanding how an ACO actually works means understanding four interlocking concepts.

**<T>Attribution</T>** is how patients get assigned to the ACO. Unlike a health plan, patients don't enroll in an ACO — they don't even know they're in one. Instead, CMS looks at claims data and asks: which primary care physician did this Medicare patient see most often over the past year? That physician's ACO gets attributed the patient.

**<T>Benchmark</T>** is what the ACO's performance gets measured against. At the start of a contract period, CMS calculates what it would have expected to spend on the ACO's attributed patients, based on their historical costs and health profiles.

**<T>Shared savings</T>** is the financial payoff for performance below the benchmark. Not all the savings flow to the ACO — CMS keeps a portion, and the ACO receives a percentage, typically between 50% and 75% depending on the track.

**Quality scoring** acts as a gate on the entire system. An ACO must meet minimum performance thresholds across a defined set of quality measures — tracked via <T>HEDIS</T> — before it can receive any shared savings.

## Governance and Economics

ACOs are legal entities — usually structured as an LLC or similar — and they require governance infrastructure: a board, an operating agreement, and a mechanism for distributing savings among participating providers.

For physicians, joining an ACO doesn't necessarily change how they practice day-to-day. They still see patients, still bill <T>fee-for-service</T>, still get paid per visit. What changes is that they now have a shared financial stake in the performance of the whole network, access to <T>population health</T> data they wouldn't otherwise see, and often a care management infrastructure — nurse navigators, care coordinators, data analysts — that the ACO maintains.

This is why the <T>MSO</T> — the Management Services Organization — became such an important part of the VBC ecosystem. Many ACOs, especially smaller physician-led ones, simply don't have the operational capacity to run this machinery on their own.

## Why ACOs Matter

ACOs remain the most widely implemented structure for provider-level accountability in US healthcare. They are the mechanism through which CMS has pushed value-based care into the mainstream — and they set the template for almost every VBC organization that followed.

Everything in the chapters ahead — <T>IPAs</T>, <T>MSOs</T>, risk-bearing entities, care management infrastructure — exists in significant part to help provider organizations succeed inside ACO-style accountability structures.
