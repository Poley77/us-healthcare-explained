If there is one organization that shapes American healthcare more than any other, it is not a hospital, a <T>health plan</T>, or a technology company. It is a federal agency most people have never heard of.

The <T>Centers for <T>Medicare</T> & <T>Medicaid</T> Services</T> (CMS) administers Medicare, sets the federal rules for Medicaid, and operates the most influential payment innovation program in the country. When CMS decides how to pay for something, or how to measure quality, or which types of organizations can participate in which programs, the entire healthcare industry adjusts.

Understanding CMS is not optional for anyone working in this space. It is foundational.

---

## What CMS Is and What It Does

CMS sits within the Department of Health and Human Services. Its core job is to administer the two largest government healthcare programs: Medicare and Medicaid. But it does far more than write checks.

CMS sets the payment rates that determine how much physicians, hospitals, and other providers are paid for services under Medicare — rates that private health plans often use as a reference point for their own contracts. It defines the quality measures that providers must report. It certifies which organizations can participate in government programs. It designs and runs the alternative payment models that are reshaping how care is delivered across the country.

When people in healthcare talk about "regulatory risk," they are usually talking about CMS. A change in CMS reimbursement policy can make or break business models, accelerate the adoption of new care delivery approaches, or overnight alter the economics of entire sectors.

## <T>Traditional Medicare</T>: The Baseline

Traditional Medicare — sometimes called Original Medicare — is the federal health insurance program created in 1965 for Americans aged 65 and older, and for younger people with qualifying disabilities.

It is organized into parts. Part A covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facilities, and some home health and hospice services. Most beneficiaries pay no premium for Part A, having paid into it through payroll taxes during their working years. Part B covers outpatient services — physician visits, preventive care, lab tests, durable medical equipment — and requires a monthly premium. Part D, added in 2003, covers prescription drugs through private plans contracted with CMS.

Traditional Medicare pays providers on a <T>fee-for-service</T> basis: a claim for each service, processed against a schedule of rates. CMS sets those rates using complex formulas, updated annually, that reflect physician work, practice costs, and regional adjustments. Hospitals are paid under separate systems for inpatient and outpatient services.

This is the baseline. Everything in <T>value-based care</T> is built on top of it — or designed as an alternative to it.

## The <T>Medicare <T>Shared Savings</T> Program</T>

The Medicare Shared Savings Program — MSSP — was created by the <T>Affordable Care Act</T> in 2010 and launched in 2012. It is the primary vehicle through which CMS has introduced ACO-style accountability into traditional Medicare.

Under MSSP, provider organizations form ACOs and apply to participate. CMS assigns attributed patients — Medicare beneficiaries who have received most of their primary care from physicians in the ACO — and sets a spending <T>benchmark</T> based on that population's historical costs. At the end of each performance period, if the ACO's actual spending comes in below the benchmark and quality thresholds are met, the ACO shares in the savings. Organizations that accept <T>downside risk</T> face financial penalties if spending runs over.

MSSP has grown substantially since its launch. There are now hundreds of participating ACOs covering millions of Medicare beneficiaries. The program has evolved through multiple iterations, with CMS progressively pushing ACOs toward higher-risk tracks and stronger accountability. The current structure — called BASIC and ENHANCED tracks — offers a glide path for newer entrants while requiring more established ACOs to accept meaningful downside risk.

MSSP remains the most widely used ACO program in the country, and for most provider organizations entering value-based care, it is where the journey begins.

## <T>ACO REACH</T>: The Next Generation

ACO REACH — Realizing Equity, Access, and Community Health — launched in 2023, replacing a prior model called Direct Contracting. It represents CMS's most ambitious attempt yet to push provider organizations toward full accountability for the <T>total cost of care</T>.

Where MSSP is primarily a shared savings program with optional downside risk, ACO REACH offers a <T>global <T>capitation</T></T> option: participating organizations can receive a fixed monthly payment per attributed beneficiary and take on full financial responsibility for their care. This is the highest level of risk in the CMS portfolio — and the highest potential reward.

ACO REACH also introduced features absent from earlier models. It requires participating organizations to develop and implement health equity plans, targeting improvements in care for underserved populations. It introduced new governance requirements designed to ensure that participating entities — including those backed by investors — maintain meaningful clinician leadership. And it opened participation to a broader set of organization types, including entities without existing Medicare billing relationships, explicitly welcoming new entrants with technology and operational capabilities.

The program attracted significant attention from investors and VBC platform companies because of its capitation option and its flexibility. It also attracted scrutiny — concerns about private equity involvement and whether the equity goals would be meaningfully pursued — which has shaped ongoing debate about how programs like REACH should be structured going forward.

## CMMI: The Innovation Engine

Running beneath MSSP and ACO REACH is an institution that most people outside healthcare policy have never encountered: the <T>Center for Medicare and Medicaid Innovation</T>, universally called CMMI.

Created by the ACA in 2010, CMMI was given a specific mandate and unusual authority: design, test, and evaluate new healthcare payment and delivery models. If a model tested by CMMI demonstrates that it reduces costs without reducing quality — or improves quality without increasing costs — CMS can expand it nationally without requiring additional legislation. This is a significant power. It means CMMI can effectively scale new payment approaches across the entire Medicare program if the evidence supports it.

CMMI has launched dozens of models over the years — some successful, some not, many still being evaluated. Beyond ACO programs, it has tested bundled payment models (paying a single fee for an episode of care like a hip replacement), primary care transformation models (paying primary care physicians differently to support more comprehensive care), and specialty care initiatives. Not every model has worked. CMMI has been candid about mixed results and has refined its approach over time.

But CMMI's importance is structural as much as programmatic. It is the federal mechanism through which payment innovation in Medicare gets tested, evaluated, and scaled. Every major VBC model operating in Medicare today either runs through CMMI or was shaped by its earlier work.

## The Broader CMS Program Landscape

MSSP, ACO REACH, and CMMI are the programs most people in VBC encounter first. But CMS administers a much wider portfolio. Here is a concise map of the rest.

<T>Medicare Advantage</T> (Part C) deserves mention here even though it gets its own chapter. MA allows private insurers to offer Medicare benefits in place of traditional Medicare, with CMS paying a fixed monthly rate per enrollee. It now covers roughly half of all Medicare beneficiaries and is the single largest growth driver in the VBC ecosystem. Chapter 12 covers it in full.

<T>Bundled Payments</T> for Care Improvement Advanced (BPCI-A) is a CMMI model that pays a single fixed price for an entire episode of care — a hip replacement, a cardiac procedure, a pneumonia hospitalization — rather than separate fees for each service. Providers that manage the episode efficiently keep the difference; those that overspend absorb the loss. It encourages coordination across hospitals, surgeons, post-acute facilities, and follow-up care in ways that fee-for-service does not.

Primary Care First (PCF) is a CMMI model designed to reward primary care practices for keeping patients healthy and out of hospitals. Practices receive a flat per-patient monthly payment plus performance bonuses, and accept financial risk if their high-risk patients are hospitalized too frequently. It is a more targeted version of the capitation logic, aimed specifically at primary care.

Comprehensive Primary Care Plus (CPC+) was an earlier CMMI primary care model that ran from 2017 to 2021, paying practices enhanced monthly fees in exchange for more comprehensive <T>care management</T>. It laid groundwork for Primary Care First and demonstrated that primary care practices could take on more structured accountability.

The Kidney Care Choices (KCC) Model focuses on patients with chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal disease — a population with very high costs and historically poor care coordination. It pays participating nephrologists and practices on a capitated basis, with incentives to delay dialysis, increase home dialysis, and improve transplant rates.

The Enhancing Oncology Model (EOM) applies a similar logic to cancer care, paying oncology practices a monthly care management fee and performance bonuses in exchange for taking on some financial risk for chemotherapy episodes. It replaced an earlier CMMI cancer care model and continues CMS's effort to bring VBC accountability to specialty care.

Medicaid is administered jointly by CMS and state governments and covers roughly 90 million lower-income Americans. While Medicaid is not a VBC program per se, most states now use Medicaid Managed Care Organizations (MCOs) — private health plans paid a capitated rate per enrollee to manage Medicaid benefits. CMS sets federal guardrails; states design their own programs within those limits. Medicaid MCOs are a major and growing part of the managed care landscape.

The Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) covers children in families whose incomes are too high for Medicaid but too low for private insurance. Like Medicaid, it is a federal-state partnership administered under CMS oversight, and like Medicaid it has increasingly moved toward managed care delivery.

The Program of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly (<T>PACE</T>) is a fully integrated care model for frail seniors who qualify for nursing home care but prefer to remain in the community. PACE organizations receive capitated payments from both Medicare and Medicaid and take on full financial responsibility for participants' healthcare. It is one of the oldest and most comprehensive integrated care models in the country, and a reference point for what full capitation can look like in practice.

## Why CMS Matters to Everyone in the Industry

Even organizations that do not directly participate in Medicare programs feel CMS's influence.

Private health plans — including Medicare Advantage plans — design their quality measurement frameworks around CMS standards. Commercial payers often follow CMS payment methodology changes. State Medicaid programs operate under federal rules set by CMS. Technology vendors build their platforms to handle CMS-required data formats and reporting requirements.

CMS is not just the administrator of government programs. It is the de facto standard-setter for the entire industry. What CMS measures, the industry learns to track. What CMS pays for, the industry learns to do. What CMS stops paying for, the industry learns to deprioritize.

Anyone serious about understanding US healthcare needs to understand CMS — not as a bureaucratic detail, but as the most powerful force shaping how care is organized, measured, and paid for in America.
